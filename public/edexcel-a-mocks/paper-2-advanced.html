Extract A
Trade tensions reshape global supply chains

The US–China trade relationship has deteriorated further in 2024–2025. The US has imposed new tariffs on Chinese EVs (100%), semiconductors (50%), solar cells (50%) and critical minerals. In response, China has imposed retaliatory tariffs on US agricultural products and restricted exports of rare earths essential to Western manufacturing. The average effective US tariff on Chinese imports has risen to over 31% in mid-2025.

Global firms have accelerated “China + 1” strategies, diversifying production to Vietnam, Mexico, and India. Vietnam’s exports to the US grew 34% in 2024. Apple shifted 20% of iPhone assembly to India. Some analysts describe this as “friend-shoring” or “near-shoring”, but others argue that much production has merely re-routed through third countries while remaining dependent on Chinese inputs.

The effects are visible in inflation data. The IMF estimates that the 2024–25 tariff rounds added 0.4 percentage points to US CPI inflation, partially reversing progress on disinflation. The tariffs’ revenue has provided the US Treasury with approximately \$80 billion annually but is widely estimated to be paid primarily by US consumers and importers rather than Chinese exporters. The US Federal Reserve has warned that further tariff escalation could delay rate cuts.

The Chinese authorities have allowed the yuan to weaken from 6.78 to the dollar in early 2023 to over 7.30 by mid-2025 — partially offsetting the impact of US tariffs on Chinese exporters. However, this risks accusations of currency manipulation and complicates China’s own efforts to support domestic consumer demand.

(Source: adapted from https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WEO/Issues/2025)

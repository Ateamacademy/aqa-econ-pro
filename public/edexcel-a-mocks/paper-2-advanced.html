Extract A
Milei's shock therapy

Javier Milei took office on 10 December 2023 facing headline CPI inflation of 211%, a fiscal deficit above 5% of GDP, negative net foreign exchange reserves, and an estimated 40% of the population below the poverty line. His early policy steps included a 54% devaluation of the official peso rate, the removal of most export taxes, the cancellation of thousands of public-sector contracts, and the elimination of a range of energy and transport subsidies.

By late 2024 Argentina had achieved a primary fiscal surplus equivalent to around 0.4% of GDP. The monthly inflation rate fell from 25.5% in December 2023 to 2.4% in December 2024 as the government tightly controlled the monetary base and reduced fiscal dominance.

(Source: adapted from Financial Times, 2025)

Extract B
The dollarisation debate

Milei's original 2023 election manifesto proposed eliminating the central bank and replacing the peso with the US dollar as Argentina's official currency. Although the plan was shelved when it became clear that Argentina's foreign-exchange reserves were inadequate to finance a credible transition, dollarisation remains a topic of debate.

Supporters argue dollarisation would permanently end Argentina's inflation problem by removing the ability of the government to monetise deficits, lock in lower borrowing costs, and restore capital inflows. Critics including IMF staff argue that Argentina's economy is too subject to commodity-price and external shocks to give up an exchange-rate adjustment mechanism permanently, and that dollarisation without fiscal discipline would merely relocate the crisis.

(Source: adapted from IMF staff working paper, 2025)

Extract C
The social cost of adjustment

The short-run cost of Milei's adjustment was severe. Argentine GDP contracted by 1.7% in 2024 as the price-level reset and fiscal consolidation squeezed real wages. Household poverty rose from 42% to a peak of 55% in the first half of 2024 before beginning to recede. Industrial production fell by 9.4% in the first half of 2024 before stabilising.

Supporters argue these costs are the price of ending a four-decade cycle of inflation and fiscal excess, and that by 2025 real wages had begun to recover as inflation slowed. Opponents argue the distributional impact has been regressive and that inequality, already high, has risen further in a pattern familiar from previous Latin American stabilisation programmes.

(Source: adapted from The Economist, 2025)

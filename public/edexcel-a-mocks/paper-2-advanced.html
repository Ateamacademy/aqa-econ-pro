Extract A
The end of the investment-led growth model

For three decades until around 2014, China's growth was sustained by very high rates of investment, much of it in infrastructure and residential property. By the peak of the investment cycle, real-estate investment alone exceeded 15% of GDP, financed through a combination of land sales by local governments, shadow-banking credit, and bank lending against rising property collateral.

The unwinding of this model, which began in 2021 with the forced deleveraging of major property developers, has exposed the scale of embedded non-performing loans and local-government financing-vehicle debts. New-home starts in 2024 were approximately 60% below the 2019 peak.

Economists including Michael Pettis have argued that the transition away from this model cannot avoid either a prolonged growth slowdown or a rise in the household consumption share of GDP that would require substantial political and institutional change.

(Source: adapted from IMF Article IV Consultation and international research, 2024)

Extract B
Deflationary pressures and the 'debt deflation' risk

CPI inflation in China fell to −0.3% by mid-2024, with producer prices falling for twenty consecutive months. Weak domestic demand, combined with rapid growth in manufacturing capacity, has generated significant excess supply in sectors from electric vehicles to solar panels, part of which has been exported aggressively to advanced and emerging economies.

Economists have warned of the risk of Irving Fisher's debt deflation dynamic: falling prices raise the real value of existing nominal debts, generating further deleveraging, falling demand and additional price declines. This dynamic is particularly concerning given that non-financial corporate debt reached 136% of GDP by 2024.

(Source: adapted from Peterson Institute for International Economics briefings, 2024)

Extract C
Policy options and international spillovers

Chinese policymakers have so far resisted calls for a large-scale household stimulus, arguing that 'welfarism' would weaken work incentives. Instead, policy has focused on easing monetary conditions and supporting targeted sectors identified as strategic priorities, including EVs, batteries and semiconductors.

Rising Chinese export volumes in these sectors have prompted both the US and EU to impose tariffs; China's trading partners have warned that persistent bilateral trade imbalances, if uncorrected, risk triggering a broader trade-policy backlash. Some commentators argue that China's deflationary pressures are effectively being exported to the rest of the world.

(Source: adapted from The Economist and OECD Economic Outlook, 2024)

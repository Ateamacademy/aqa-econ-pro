General Marking Guidance

All candidates must receive the same treatment. Examiners must mark the first candidate in exactly the same way as they mark the last. Mark schemes should be applied positively. Candidates must be rewarded for what they have shown they can do rather than penalised for omissions.

Question 2 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Fiscal consolidation reduces G component of AD → AD shifts left.
  • Lower AD → lower output and lower price level in short run.
  • If coupled with credibility effect: inflation expectations fall; SRAS shifts right → mitigates output loss.
  • Argentina 2024: GDP fell 1.7%, inflation fell from 211% to 42%.
Answer: B
Ricardian equivalence: consumers anticipate future tax rises needed to service government borrowing → save instead of spend.

Question 3 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Full dollarisation means Argentina loses ability to issue its own currency.
  • Trilemma: with free capital flows and fixed exchange rate (dollarisation is 'hardest peg'), monetary policy cannot adjust to domestic conditions.
  • Argentina inherits US monetary policy wholesale.
  • Diagram reasoning: two apices of triangle chosen → third must be sacrificed.
Answer: B
Seigniorage = profit from issuing currency above production cost; dollarisation transfers this to the US Federal Reserve.

Question 4 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Short-run cost: output fall, unemployment rises; regressive distributional impact.
  • Long-run benefit: lower inflation; reduced risk premium; better investment climate.
  • Credibility gained cannot be easily recreated if lost.
  • Argentina 2024: poverty rose to 55% in H1 but inflation fell dramatically.
Answer: B
Non-Keynesian effects: when fiscal consolidation improves credibility, lower risk premia and confidence effects can dominate contractionary effect.

Question 5 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Benefit: external commitment device; reduces time-inconsistency problem; unlocks capital inflows.
  • Cost: political sovereignty loss; conditions may be ill-suited to country context (one-size-fits-all critique).
  • Moral hazard: may encourage risky fiscal behaviour in expectation of bailout.
Answer: A
Moral hazard = insurance changing risk-taking behaviour; IMF lending can encourage excessive pre-crisis borrowing.

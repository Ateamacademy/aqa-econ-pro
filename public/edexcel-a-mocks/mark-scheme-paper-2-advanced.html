Question 1 — Taylor rule calculation

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
1(a)Taylor rule: i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(y − y*).
Substituting: r* = 2, π = 4.5, π* = 2, (y − y*) = 1.2. i = 2 + 4.5 + 0.5(4.5 − 2) + 0.5(1.2) = 2 + 4.5 + 0.5(2.5) + 0.6 = 6.5 + 1.25 + 0.6 = 8.35%.
  • Difference from pure IT rule: Taylor rule reacts to both inflation and output gap, giving weight to real-economy stabilisation.
  • Pure IT rule would only respond to the π − π* term; Taylor rule includes (y − y*) term.

Question 2 — Monetary transmission lags

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
2(a)Why lags are a real-time challenge:
  • Policy changes today affect output in 18–24 months; affect inflation at ~24 months.
  • Real-time data is noisy and heavily revised (GDP revisions sometimes ±1 pp).
  • Output-gap and NAIRU estimates revised ex-post.
  • Requires policy to be forward-looking, based on forecasts — but forecast errors significant.
  • Mistiming risk: overtightening into a cycle peak, or easing late into a recession.
  • Classic Friedman 'long and variable lags' argument.

Question 3 — Ricardian equivalence

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
3(a)Two reasons Ricardian equivalence fails in practice:
  • Finite horizons: households may die before future tax increase arrives; unless bequest-linked (Barro infinite-horizon), households underweight future tax.
  • Liquidity constraints: borrowing-constrained households cannot smooth consumption; a tax cut today raises current C even if future tax is anticipated.
  • Myopia / bounded rationality: households do not fully forecast implied future taxes.
  • Distortionary taxes: future taxes have different deadweight costs than current taxes, breaking equivalence.
  • Uncertainty over future tax policy: discounts expected future tax claim.
L11One reason identified.
L22–3Chain of reasoning.
L34Two fully developed reasons.
4AO1/AO3
3(b)B — households have infinite planning horizons and are unconstrained. Barro's restatement of Ricardo assumes infinitely-lived (or bequest-linked) unconstrained households — without this, equivalence fails.1AO1

Question 4 — Hysteresis

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
4(a)One mechanism of hysteresis:
  • Skills atrophy: prolonged unemployment erodes human capital → lower MRPL.
  • Insider-outsider dynamics: employed 'insiders' negotiate wages oblivious to 'outsider' unemployed pool; wage pressure continues at higher unemployment rate.
  • Stigma effect: long-unemployed workers screened out by employers; become effectively outside the active labour force.
  • Loss of networks: employment often obtained via networks that decay when out of work.
  • Mechanism effect: NAIRU (equilibrium unemployment) rises after a demand shock.

Question 7 (EITHER) — Accelerated net-zero transition: greenflation

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
7Evaluate macroeconomic consequences:
  • Climateflation / greenflation: carbon pricing and reg. shock raise energy / input prices → supply-side inflation.
  • Fossilflation: uneven transition with under-investment in fossil capacity → energy-price spikes.
  • Output: short-run negative (SRAS shifts left from carbon-intensive capital scrappage); long-run positive as green productivity improves.
  • Distributional: regressive burden of carbon prices on low-income households; political economy challenge.
  • Labour market: re-skilling challenge; structural unemployment in carbon-intensive regions (Lancashire, Ruhr).
  • Financial stability: stranded-asset risk (fossil-fuel reserves, coal power plants) → sudden asset revaluations.
  • Investment boom: green-transition CAPEX could raise AD, close output gaps.
  • Monetary policy: CBs forced to 'look through' supply-side inflation (like 1970s) — credibility test.
  • International: unilateral action creates competitiveness issues; CBAM attempts mitigation.
  • Judgement: accelerated transition raises short-run macroeconomic costs but may be net-positive long-run if accompanied by credible investment programme.

Question 8 (OR) — Dollar dominance and the Triffin dilemma

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
8Evaluate dollar dominance:
  • Triffin dilemma: reserve-currency issuer must supply world with liquidity (running current-account deficits) while maintaining confidence in currency value → contradictory demands.
  • US position: 'exorbitant privilege' (Giscard) — cheap funding, safe-haven demand during crises.
  • Global financial cycle (Rey): US rate changes transmit globally regardless of receiving country's exchange-rate regime.
  • Consequences: volatility in capital flows to EMs during US tightening; EM currency crises.
  • Alternative reserve currencies: euro limited by EU fiscal fragmentation; yuan limited by capital controls / political risk.
  • Emerging alternatives: BRICS settlement arrangements; SDR expansion; but none can scale to dollar levels yet.
  • De-dollarisation trends: China-Russia trade in yuan; CBDC projects; selective decoupling post-sanctions.
  • Judgement: instability real but genuine alternative absent; reform likely gradual (multi-polar reserve system) rather than revolutionary.

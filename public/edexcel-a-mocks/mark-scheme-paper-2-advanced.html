Question 6(b) – Examine two factors that may explain why the US imposed higher tariffs on Chinese imports (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Trade deficit concerns: US has persistent bilateral goods deficit with China (~\$300bn); tariffs intended to narrow the deficit (though the economics literature disputes effectiveness).
  • Strategic / national security: semiconductors, critical minerals, telecoms (Huawei/ZTE). Tariffs plus export controls used to slow Chinese technological catch-up.
  • Reciprocity argument: Chinese IP protection, forced technology transfer and state subsidies for strategic industries distort competition; tariffs as retaliation for non-tariff barriers.
  • Political economy: swing-state manufacturing jobs; “China shock” research suggests 2.0–2.4m US manufacturing jobs lost to Chinese competition 2000–2015.
  • Fiscal revenue: \$80bn/year to Treasury — small in absolute terms but politically salient.
  • Green industrial policy: EV and solar tariffs protect nascent US clean-tech industry.

Award 8-mark "Examine" band (KAA = 5, Eval = 3).

Question 6(c) – Assess the likely impact of the US tariffs on US consumer welfare (10 marks)

KAA (out of 6)
  • Tariff diagram: domestic price rises by tariff amount (if imported supply perfectly elastic); consumer surplus falls; producer surplus rises for domestic producers; government collects tariff revenue; deadweight loss (efficiency triangles).
  • IMF estimate: 0.4pp added to US CPI — higher prices borne by consumers.
  • Incidence: evidence suggests tariffs passed through to consumers rather than absorbed by Chinese exporters.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Short-run: clear consumer welfare loss via higher prices on affected goods.
  • Long-run: if tariffs succeed in reviving US manufacturing employment, affected workers gain — but small share of total consumers.
  • Distributional: low-income consumers spend more of income on tariffed goods (electronics, clothing, household items) — regressive.
  • Offsetting policies: Treasury tariff revenue could fund redistribution but typically has not.
  • Dynamic effects: protection may reduce innovation pressure on domestic producers.
  • Judgement: on balance, net consumer welfare impact is negative in the short to medium term; some distributional offsets via higher manufacturing wages for a minority of workers, but broad-based consumer cost.

Question 6(d) – Discuss, using an AD/AS diagram, the likely impact of the depreciation of the yuan on the Chinese macroeconomy (12 marks)

Diagram required: AD shift right and potentially SRAS shift left on the same diagram.

KAA with diagram (out of 8)
  • AD/AS: yuan depreciation makes Chinese exports cheaper in foreign currency → X rises; imports more expensive → M falls; net exports rise → AD shifts right.
  • However, depreciation also raises imported input costs (oil, semiconductors, food) → SRAS may shift left.
  • Application: ~8% depreciation partially offsets new US tariffs on Chinese exports.
  • Chinese exporters in tariff-exposed sectors benefit; Chinese consumers face higher prices for imports.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Effectiveness depends on Marshall-Lerner: sum of PEDs > 1. For China, likely met given elastic export demand.
  • Risk of currency manipulation accusations and further tariff escalation.
  • Capital outflow pressure: depreciation can trigger speculative attacks (China learned from 2015 devaluation).
  • Imported inflation counteracts the PBoC's attempts to support demand.
  • Judgement: depreciation provides short-term relief for exporters but creates inflationary pressure domestically and geopolitical friction; it is a partial, not complete, response to tariffs.

Question 6(e) – Discuss whether "friend-shoring" and supply-chain diversification are likely to benefit emerging economies such as Vietnam, Mexico and India (15 marks)

KAA (out of 9)

Benefits:

  • Export growth (Vietnam to US +34% in 2024).
  • FDI inflows and technology transfer.
  • Manufacturing employment growth.
  • Integration into global value chains.
  • Diversification of trade partners.

Costs / limitations:

  • Much “re-routing” still uses Chinese inputs — limited genuine industrial upgrading.
  • Upward pressure on wages and currencies can erode competitiveness.
  • Infrastructure strain (ports, electricity).
  • Dependence on US demand — vulnerable to US recession or trade policy shifts.
  • Technology transfer often limited (Apple in India assembles but doesn't design).
Evaluation (out of 6)
  • Short-run: clear growth boost for host economies; Vietnam GDP growth ~6%, India ~7% partly driven by this.
  • Long-run: benefits depend on whether hosts can move up value chain from assembly to design and IP-intensive activities.
  • Institutional quality matters: Vietnam and India face governance challenges that may limit gains.
  • Global trade fragmentation may reduce total trade volumes — relative winners in a shrinking pie.
  • China's retaliation (restricting rare-earth exports) affects friend-shore hosts indirectly.
  • Judgement: friend-shoring offers genuine development opportunity for capable recipient economies but is not costless; long-run gains depend on recipient policy capacity to convert assembly into genuine industrial upgrading. Risk of “middle-income trap” if stuck at low-value assembly.

Section C — Question 7 OR Question 8 (25 marks)

Question 7 – Evaluate the likely macroeconomic consequences of rising public debt for a developed country of your choice (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Public debt definitions: gross vs. net; denominated in domestic or foreign currency.
  • Debt dynamics: d(Debt/GDP)/dt = primary deficit + (r − g) × Debt/GDP.
  • Crowding-out: higher debt → higher interest rates → lower private investment.
  • Sovereign risk premium: higher debt → higher yields required.
  • Fiscal space: debt limits future countercyclical policy capacity.
  • Application (UK ~99% GDP, US ~122%, Italy ~140%, Japan ~250%): contrasts in market reactions.
  • Diagram: loanable funds model showing government borrowing shifting demand for funds → higher r.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Sustainability depends on r vs. g, not just the debt level.
  • Reserve-currency issuer (US) faces different constraints than peripheral eurozone members.
  • Short-run: countercyclical fiscal policy may be worth the debt cost during recession.
  • Long-run: debt service crowds out productive spending (UK £105bn debt interest vs. £80bn defence budget).
  • Intergenerational equity concerns.
  • Dependency on creditor confidence — can shift rapidly (Greece 2010, UK Sept 2022).
  • Judgement: debt is not intrinsically bad but becomes so when r > g persistently, when fiscal stance is pro-cyclical in booms, or when it erodes policy space needed for future shocks. For reserve-currency issuers the threshold is higher but not infinite.

Question 8 – Evaluate the likely macroeconomic consequences of a significant appreciation of a country's currency (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Effect on net exports: X falls (exports more expensive in foreign currency), M rises → AD falls.
  • Effect on inflation: imported goods cheaper → imported disinflation.
  • Effect on domestic competitiveness: exporters' profit margins squeezed.
  • Effect on capital flows: attracts capital seeking higher real returns.
  • Marshall-Lerner condition for effect on trade balance.
  • Application: US dollar +15% since 2020, driven by interest rate differentials and safe-haven flows.
  • Diagram: AD/AS with AD leftward; or exchange-rate diagram showing appreciation.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Short-run: damages export-reliant sectors (US manufacturing); benefits consumers via lower import prices.
  • Long-run: sustained appreciation may force restructuring toward services (Dutch disease possibility).
  • Monetary policy: appreciation acts like tighter policy; central bank may cut rates to offset.
  • Distributional: export workers lose, import-dependent industries and consumers gain.
  • International: dollar strength = stress for EM dollar-debtors.
  • Judgement: impact depends heavily on cause of appreciation (fundamentals vs. speculation) and duration. Short-run appreciation driven by sound macro policy (like US 2022–24) is largely absorbed; prolonged overvaluation leads to structural damage. Policy response should focus on supply-side competitiveness rather than intervention.

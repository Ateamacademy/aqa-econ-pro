Extract A
The bursting of the 1990 bubble

Between 1985 and 1990 Japanese equity and commercial real-estate prices rose by over 300%. When the asset bubble burst in 1990–91, both equity and property prices fell by more than 60% over the subsequent decade, wiping out approximately US$16 trillion of private-sector wealth.

Japanese firms and households responded with sustained efforts to repay debt rather than take on new borrowing, even as the Bank of Japan reduced interest rates towards zero. The economist Richard Koo described this as a balance-sheet recession: an economy-wide prioritisation of debt reduction over new investment, which conventional monetary policy is poorly equipped to address.

GDP growth averaged just 1.0% over 1992–2012 and CPI inflation averaged near zero, with multiple episodes of outright deflation.

(Source: adapted from Koo, R. 'The Holy Grail of Macroeconomics', and IMF data)

Extract B
The liquidity trap and unconventional monetary policy

By 1999 the Bank of Japan policy rate had reached effectively zero, yet inflation remained below target. Under these conditions, further conventional cuts become impossible, and the economy is said to be caught in a liquidity trap.

The Bank of Japan responded with a series of unconventional measures: quantitative easing (QE) from 2001, forward guidance, negative interest rates on reserves from 2016, and yield-curve control from 2016 — under which the ten-year government bond yield was explicitly targeted at approximately zero percent.

Critics argued these measures distorted the financial system, impaired the profitability of commercial banks, and made the eventual normalisation of policy extremely difficult. Supporters pointed to a return of positive inflation in 2023–2024 as evidence of the programme's success.

(Source: adapted from Bank of Japan and Brookings Institution papers, 2024)

Extract C
Abenomics and structural reform

In 2012 Prime Minister Shinzo Abe launched 'Abenomics', consisting of three 'arrows': aggressive monetary easing, flexible fiscal policy, and supply-side structural reforms including greater female labour-force participation, corporate-governance reform and participation in international trade agreements.

The third arrow — structural reform — remains the most contested. Critics argue insufficient progress has been made on labour-market reform, especially in breaking down the rigid distinction between permanent and non-permanent employment that constrains wage growth.

(Source: adapted from OECD Japan Economic Survey, 2024)

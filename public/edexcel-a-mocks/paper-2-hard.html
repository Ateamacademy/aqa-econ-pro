Extract A
Erdogan's unorthodox experiment

Between 2021 and mid-2023 President Erdogan imposed what economists call an "unorthodox" monetary framework on Turkey's central bank, arguing — against textbook economics — that high interest rates cause high inflation rather than curbing it. Four governors of the Central Bank of the Republic of Turkey were dismissed in as many years for resisting rate cuts.

The policy produced an inflation spiral: as the real interest rate turned sharply negative, households pulled lira deposits and bought dollars, gold and real estate. The lira lost around three-quarters of its value against the dollar between 2020 and 2023, and headline CPI inflation peaked at 85.5% in October 2022.

(Source: adapted from The Economist, 2025)

Extract B
Return to orthodoxy

Following the May 2023 election Erdogan appointed Mehmet Şimşek as finance minister and former First Vice Governor of the Fed Hafize Gaye Erkan as central bank governor. The bank raised rates from 8.5% to 42.5% by end-2023 and to 50% by March 2024, the largest and fastest hiking cycle in Turkey's history.

Inflation expectations have only partially adjusted. Household surveys continued to show one-year-ahead expectations above 40% well into 2024. Economists argue that rebuilding central-bank credibility after it has been lost may take a decade of consistent behaviour, not a few quarters of orthodox policy.

(Source: adapted from Financial Times, 2025)

Extract C
A currency-board proposal

Some Turkish economists and opposition politicians have argued that, given the difficulty of rebuilding credibility, Turkey should adopt a currency board pegging the lira to the euro — similar to Hong Kong's arrangement with the US dollar or Bulgaria's with the euro.

Supporters argue a currency board would anchor inflation expectations and restore capital inflows. Critics counter that Turkey's economy is too large and too asymmetric to the eurozone for a peg to be credible, that the loss of an independent exchange-rate policy would amplify shocks, and that Turkey would need substantial reserves to defend the peg during crises.

(Source: adapted from Brookings Institution paper, 2025)

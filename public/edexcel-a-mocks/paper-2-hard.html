Extract A
Transition challenge for developing economies

The transition to net zero presents profound challenges for developing and emerging economies. Many remain heavily dependent on fossil fuel imports (India, Vietnam, South Africa) or on fossil fuel exports (Saudi Arabia, Nigeria, Algeria). At the same time, they have typically contributed least to cumulative emissions but suffer disproportionately from physical climate impacts — heat stress, drought, cyclones, sea-level rise.

The 2009 Copenhagen pledge of \$100 billion per year in climate finance for developing countries was finally met in 2022, two years late. A new collective finance goal agreed at COP29 in 2024 sets a target of \$300 billion annually by 2035, but emerging economy negotiators argued that actual needs are closer to \$1 trillion per year. Private finance has grown but largely flows to middle-income economies with established capital markets; the least-developed countries remain dependent on concessional public finance.

Debt dynamics are severe. Over 50% of low-income countries are at high risk of debt distress in 2025, and many face the “triple shock” of tight global financial conditions, climate-change physical risks, and stranded-asset risks for fossil-fuel economies. The IMF estimates that bringing emerging market electricity generation to net zero by 2050 requires roughly \$2 trillion per year in investment from 2030.

South Africa’s Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP), announced at COP26 with \$8.5 billion of finance commitments, has been held up as a model. However, implementation has been slow, held back by local political tensions, an ageing coal fleet that generates 80% of electricity, and limited grid infrastructure to absorb renewables.

(Source: adapted from https://www.worldbank.org/climate-and-development/finance)

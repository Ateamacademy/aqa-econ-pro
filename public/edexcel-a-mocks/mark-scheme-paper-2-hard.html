Question 2 — Persistent negative output gap

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
2(a)Why economy may not self-correct to potential:
  • Sticky wages / prices: money illusion, implicit contracts, menu costs — slow adjustment of W/P downwards.
  • AD-AS diagram: AD below ASLR; neoclassical self-correction requires P ↓ to stimulate real money balances and wealth effect — slow if deflation avoided.
  • Hysteresis: long unemployment erodes skills/work habits → ASLR shifts left, preventing return to original potential.
  • Liquidity trap at near-zero rates: conventional monetary policy cannot stimulate AD back to potential.
  • Pessimistic expectations: if firms expect weak demand they defer I; self-fulfilling stagnation.

Question 5 — Secular stagnation

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
5(a)Savings glut → secular stagnation:
  • High savings in EM economies (China, oil exporters) → desired global savings exceed desired global investment.
  • Equilibrium real interest rate (r*) falls — loanable-funds diagram: rightward S shift → r* ↓.
  • If r* is negative at full employment, CB cannot achieve it via nominal rate cuts due to ZLB.
  • Result: persistent negative output gap in advanced economies.
  • Summers-style argument: demographic aging compounds effect.

Question 7 (EITHER) — MMT and UK policy

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
7Evaluate MMT claim:
  • MMT claim: sovereign issuer of its own currency cannot 'run out of money'; constraint is real resources / inflation.
  • Implications: deficit per se not a problem; focus on capacity utilisation.
  • Supporting evidence: Japan's high debt coexisting with low inflation and low yields; US large-deficit period.
  • Criticisms: inflation constraint hard to detect in real time; monetary-fiscal coordination risks CB credibility loss.
  • Bond-market consequences: if investors lose confidence, yields spike — see UK mini-budget 2022 episode.
  • Exchange-rate consequences: currency depreciation → imported inflation.
  • UK-specific: open economy, large external debt, reserve-currency status less than USD → less flexibility than US.
  • Operational accuracy of MMT description (money creation via bank reserves) is broadly correct; policy recommendation more contested.
  • Judgement: MMT's operational description partially correct; policy prescriptions risky for UK context; inflation constraint binding in practice.

Question 8 (OR) — Central-bank independence coordination

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
8Evaluate coordination vs independence:
  • CB independence benefits: lower average inflation (Alesina-Summers 1993), anchored expectations, time-consistency solution.
  • Costs of separation: in crises, fiscal and monetary may need to align (QE + fiscal stimulus 2009, 2020).
  • Current UK framework: BoE has operational independence, HMT sets the target; APF (QE) has Treasury indemnity — already a form of coordination.
  • Explicit coordination proposals: 'fiscal-monetary coordination' (Borio) — joint policy path.
  • Risks: fiscal dominance; erosion of CB credibility; inflation-targeting regime undermined.
  • Supply-shock era: pure inflation targeting cycling into pro-cyclical policy a concern; formal coordination might mitigate.
  • Alternative: keep independence, improve communication (forward guidance), flexible IT.
  • Judgement: formalising coordination risks more than it gains; better to retain independence with improved framework flexibility.

Question 6(b) – Examine two factors that may explain why China has far higher installed renewable capacity than other economies (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • State-led industrial policy: Chinese five-year plans directed capital and subsidies to solar and wind manufacturing since the 2000s; production scale drove down global panel prices by over 90% since 2010.
  • Domestic manufacturing dominance: China produces 80% of global solar panels and 60% of wind components; vertical integration from polysilicon to panel assembly.
  • Grid investment: ultra-high-voltage DC transmission lines move renewable generation from interior to coastal load centres — scale investment no other country has matched.
  • Land availability: deserts in Xinjiang and Inner Mongolia for utility-scale solar and wind farms.
  • Energy security motive: reducing dependence on imported oil and coal.
  • Financial model: state-owned utilities buy at guaranteed tariffs; state banks provide cheap capital; risk transferred to state rather than private investors.

Award 8-mark "Examine" band (KAA = 5, Eval = 3).

Question 6(c) – Assess the likely impact of rising global climate finance on economic development in a low-income country of your choice (10 marks)

KAA (out of 6)
  • Climate finance channels: concessional loans, grants, blended finance, private capital.
  • Development impact: energy access (most LICs have low electrification), climate adaptation (floods, drought resilience), green jobs, transport infrastructure.
  • Multiplier effects through infrastructure investment.
  • Intersects with SDGs 1 (poverty), 7 (energy), 13 (climate).
  • Application (e.g. Kenya, Bangladesh, Senegal): specific projects and impacts.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Absorptive capacity: can the country effectively use large finance inflows? Institutional capacity matters.
  • Debt sustainability: concessional finance may still add to debt burden (over half of LICs near distress).
  • Dutch disease risk: large inflows can appreciate currency, hurt other exports.
  • “Climate finance” may be re-counted from existing aid budgets — not truly additional.
  • Conditionality: finance often tied to policy reforms not always development-aligned.
  • Local benefits depend on supplier policies — if foreign firms do all construction, local value-added is limited.
  • Judgement: climate finance offers real development opportunities for capable LICs but requires substantial absorptive capacity and risks being a mixed blessing if it crowds out other aid or comes with costly conditions. Concessional public finance more likely to aid development than private finance, which targets middle-income bankable projects.

Question 6(d) – Discuss, using an AD/AS diagram, the likely short-run macroeconomic impact of a large-scale "green industrial" investment programme on a developing economy such as South Africa (12 marks)

Diagram required: AD/AS with AD shifting right; LRAS may shift right over time.

KAA with diagram (out of 8)
  • AD/AS: large-scale investment (I↑) and government spending (G↑) raise AD.
  • If investment raises productive capacity over time, LRAS shifts right; in short run, supply constraints (skilled labour, equipment) may limit SRAS response.
  • Show new equilibrium: higher Y, higher P in short run; LRAS shift eventually allows growth without additional inflation.
  • Application: South Africa's \$8.5bn JETP commitment + broader green investment could add 2–3% to GDP annually.
  • Structural unemployment at 33% means significant slack — big output gains possible.
  • Coal sector employs ~90,000 directly in Mpumalanga — transition costs concentrated locally.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Short-run macro gain likely substantial given high unemployment and slack.
  • Long-run gain depends on successful grid reform (Eskom), labour retraining, institutional capacity.
  • Fiscal: South Africa's debt/GDP at 75%+ constrains government co-investment headroom.
  • Risks: project delays, capacity constraints, coal-sector political opposition.
  • Currency: large investment inflow could appreciate rand, hurting other exporters.
  • Judgement: green investment is strongly positive for South Africa in aggregate terms but materially positive impact requires reforms to Eskom, grid investment, and social compact with coal communities; execution risk is the binding constraint, not financing.

Question 6(e) – Discuss whether the climate transition is likely to widen or narrow the gap between developed and developing economies (15 marks)

KAA (out of 9)

Arguments the transition WIDENS the gap:

  • Physical climate impacts fall disproportionately on tropical and coastal developing economies.
  • Developed countries have fiscal headroom for mitigation investment; LICs don't.
  • Stranded-asset risk concentrated in fossil-fuel-dependent developing economies.
  • Technology leadership (EVs, batteries, solar) dominated by developed/China — rents flow there.
  • Carbon border adjustments (EU CBAM) penalise emissions-intensive developing country exports.
  • Climate finance falls far short of need (\$158bn actual vs. \$1tn estimated).

Arguments the transition NARROWS the gap:

  • Falling solar costs democratise energy access — LICs can leapfrog centralised grids.
  • Lower existing energy infrastructure reduces transition cost per capita.
  • Green industrial policy creates opportunities (Vietnam solar, India green hydrogen).
  • Labour cost advantages in green manufacturing.
  • Decentralised renewable power suited to dispersed rural populations.
Evaluation (out of 6)
  • Distinction between physical climate impacts (widening), mitigation costs (variable), and green growth opportunities (potentially narrowing).
  • Country-level variation: capable middle-income economies (India, Vietnam, Indonesia, Brazil) may benefit; low-income and climate-vulnerable countries (Bangladesh, Malawi, Haiti) suffer.
  • Institutional quality and absorptive capacity determine which side of the ledger countries end up on.
  • Role of multilateral finance and governance: WTO rules, climate finance architecture, COP negotiations.
  • Judgement: the climate transition will likely WIDEN the gap on average, but with substantial heterogeneity — capable middle-income economies may benefit while poor and climate-vulnerable economies face a triple burden of physical damage, transition costs, and limited finance. Without substantial reform of climate finance architecture, the net effect is inequality-widening.

Section C — Question 7 OR Question 8 (25 marks)

Question 7 – Evaluate the factors that may cause an emerging economy to enter debt distress (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Definitions: debt distress, debt sustainability, debt restructuring.
  • Debt dynamics: d(Debt/GDP) = primary deficit + (r − g) × Debt/GDP + exchange rate effect.
  • Drivers: fiscal (persistent primary deficits, pro-cyclical spending), monetary (high interest rates), external (commodity price shocks, global recession, sudden stops), structural (weak tax base, narrow export base).
  • International context: rising global rates post-2022, US dollar strength, capital outflows from EMs.
  • Application: Sri Lanka 2022 (tourism collapse + fertiliser policy + tax cuts → default); Zambia 2020 (copper price fall + Chinese debt); Ghana 2022 (fiscal deficit + commodity exposure).
  • Diagram: loanable funds showing sovereign spread widening; or AD/AS with negative output shock.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Distinguish solvency crisis (fundamentals) from liquidity crisis (temporary).
  • Role of creditors: Chinese lending vs. Paris Club vs. private bondholders creates coordination problems.
  • Domestic institutional factors: fiscal framework, independent central bank, currency regime.
  • Restructuring vs. austerity: social costs vs. credit-market re-access speed.
  • IMF role and conditionality.
  • Judgement: debt distress typically emerges from a combination of domestic policy failures (fiscal, monetary) and external shocks (commodity prices, global rates); the specific mix varies. Good institutions (independent central bank, counter-cyclical fiscal framework) dramatically reduce distress probability. The current rise in distress reflects the compounding of tight global conditions with pre-existing domestic vulnerabilities exposed by COVID.

Question 8 – Evaluate the most effective strategies a developing country might adopt to promote economic development (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Growth vs. development distinction.
  • Strategies: export-led (East Asian model), import-substitution (Latin American model), structural adjustment, Washington Consensus, developmental state model.
  • Sector priorities: agriculture, industry, services.
  • Investment: physical capital (infrastructure), human capital (education, health), institutional capital.
  • Trade: openness benefits vs. industrial policy for infant industries.
  • Governance: rule of law, corruption, property rights (Acemoglu-Robinson).
  • Financial development, microfinance, mobile money (M-Pesa).
  • Application: Vietnam since 1986 Doi Moi, Rwanda since 1994, Bangladesh since 2000.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • No single model: “what works” depends on initial conditions, geography, governance, external environment.
  • Export-led growth harder in fragmented world trade (post-2017 tariff rises).
  • Infrastructure investment effective only with institutional capacity.
  • Aid vs. trade vs. FDI debate — multiple channels, context-dependent.
  • Political economy: strategies must be politically sustainable.
  • Judgement: effective development strategies typically combine (a) pragmatic openness to trade and FDI, (b) investment in human capital (especially female education), (c) stable macro policy, (d) institutional reform to enable property rights and contract enforcement, (e) targeted industrial policy in niches of comparative advantage. “One-size-fits-all” fails; context-specific strategy matters more than choosing between theoretical camps.

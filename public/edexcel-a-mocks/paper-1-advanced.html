Extract A
Network effects and the self-reinforcing nature of platform power

Leading digital platforms benefit from powerful network effects: each additional user makes the platform more valuable to other users, drivers, merchants or advertisers. This creates positive feedback loops that can allow a dominant firm to entrench its position even in the absence of strong economies of scale. In several markets, two-sided network effects — between riders and drivers, buyers and sellers, or content creators and consumers — reinforce these dynamics.

Critics argue that such markets exhibit a 'winner-takes-most' tendency, and that traditional measures of market share based on revenue may understate the extent of market power. Defenders of large platforms counter that they remain contestable, citing the rapid rise of challengers such as TikTok and the disruption of legacy retailers.

Commentators have increasingly focused on non-price dimensions of competition: quality of recommendations, access to user data, and the design of interfaces that shape consumer choice.

(Source: adapted from OECD Digital Economy Outlook, 2024)

Extract B
Data as a barrier to entry

A central feature of the modern platform economy is the role of consumer data. Large incumbents have access to vast troves of behavioural, location and transaction data that allow them to personalise recommendations and target advertising with unparalleled precision. New entrants face a classic chicken-and-egg problem: without scale they cannot train effective algorithms, but without effective algorithms they cannot achieve scale.

Data portability obligations — requirements that incumbents make it easier for users to export their data to rival services — are one proposed remedy. Others include mandated interoperability, so that users of one social network can interact with users of another. Critics warn that such remedies risk diluting the product improvements that user data makes possible, and may raise privacy risks if not carefully designed.

(Source: adapted from the Stigler Center report on digital platforms, 2024)

Extract C
The UK's Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act, 2024

The UK's Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act, which came into force in late 2024, creates a new competition regime targeted at the largest digital platforms. The CMA can designate firms with 'Strategic Market Status' and impose tailored conduct requirements: non-discrimination rules, data-sharing obligations, or restrictions on self-preferencing.

Supporters argue the regime allows faster and more flexible intervention than traditional antitrust, which has often been criticised as too slow to keep up with fast-moving digital markets. Critics worry that conduct requirements may deter innovation, penalise success, and hand substantial discretionary power to a single regulator.

(Source: adapted from CMA guidance and UK government consultation papers, 2024)

Extract A
Nvidia's commanding lead

Nvidia's dominance of the AI-chip market has become one of the defining economic stories of the 2020s. The company's data-centre revenue rose from $15 billion in 2022 to over $128 billion in 2025, giving it an estimated 82% share of AI accelerator sales. Its gross margins of around 75% are among the highest in any large industry.

This position rests on three pillars: a decade-old software ecosystem (CUDA) which represents very high switching costs for customers; unrivalled performance per watt on the most demanding AI workloads; and privileged access to the leading-edge manufacturing capacity at TSMC. Competitors including AMD and startups such as Cerebras have made technical progress but struggle to break the software moat.

(Source: adapted from The Economist, 2025)

Extract B
Friend-shoring semiconductors

Fearing over-concentration of cutting-edge chip manufacturing in Taiwan, the US and its allies have subsidised new fabs in Arizona, Japan and Germany under the CHIPS Act, the Japanese CHIPS plan and the European Chips Act. Together these programmes have committed more than $100 billion in public support since 2022.

Critics argue the subsidies tilt markets and risk corporate welfare without changing the underlying economics: a leading-edge fab requires capex of around $25 billion and a continuous flow of thousands of specialist engineers. Supporters counter that the strategic resilience gained — in the event of a Taiwan Strait crisis — justifies the cost.

(Source: adapted from Financial Times, 2025)

Extract C
Export controls & innovation

Since 2022 the US has progressively tightened export controls on advanced AI chips and semiconductor manufacturing equipment to China. The 2023 rules effectively cut off Chinese buyers from H100-class Nvidia chips and from the leading-edge ASML EUV lithography tools.

Chinese firms including Huawei have accelerated domestic alternatives, launching the Ascend 910B AI chip and investing heavily in SMIC's 7nm process. Western economists debate whether export controls will slow Chinese AI progress meaningfully, or merely accelerate the bifurcation of the global technology stack. Some argue the controls risk driving Chinese firms up the innovation curve faster than they would otherwise have climbed.

(Source: adapted from The Wall Street Journal, 2025)

Question 3 — Oligopoly game theory (payoff matrix)

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
3(a)Nash equilibrium: (A: Cut, B: Cut) = (4, 4).
  • Cutting is the dominant strategy for both firms: given B maintains, A prefers 11 > 8; given B cuts, A prefers 4 > 2. By symmetry, B's dominant strategy is also Cut.
  • Jointly profit-maximising outcome: (Maintain, Maintain) = (8, 8), Pareto-superior to (4, 4).
  • Classic prisoner's dilemma: individual rationality produces a collectively inferior outcome.
  • Without binding agreement (illegal cartel), firms cannot commit to the cooperative outcome.

Question 5 — Cloud computing: egress fees as barriers

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
5(a)Indicative content — egress fees as barrier to exit:
  • Egress fees = per-GB charges to transfer stored data out of the incumbent provider.
  • Raise the switching cost: total migration cost = egress fees + retraining / reconfiguration + downtime risk.
  • Create lock-in: once data accumulates, switching becomes economically infeasible even if rival providers offer lower headline prices.
  • Effectively raises the incumbent's pricing power (reduces price elasticity of demand from locked-in customers).
  • May deter entry by reducing the addressable contestable market for new rivals.

Question 7 (EITHER) — Nudge vs Pigouvian tax for health externalities

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
7Evaluate nudges vs Pigouvian taxation:
  • Pigouvian tax: internalises externality, raises price, reduces quantity via standard tax-incidence logic; generates revenue.
  • Nudges: exploit bounded rationality (default effects, salience, framing) to shift choices without restricting them — 'libertarian paternalism'.
  • Nudges often cheaper, more politically palatable, avoid regressive tax burden.
  • Effectiveness depends on size of effect: evidence from auto-enrolment, organ donation strong; obesity evidence mixed.
  • Taxes have a stronger theoretical foundation when private marginal cost ≠ social marginal cost.
  • Hybrid approach usually optimal: tax to internalise externality + nudge to correct residual behavioural failures.
  • Equity: taxes regressive; nudges more neutral but may be paternalistic.
  • Case study for evaluation: UK sugar tax (SDIL) reduced sugar content via reformulation — a different channel again.
  • Judgement: context-dependent; tax preferable where price responsiveness high and externality measurable; nudge preferable where bias is the core failure.

Question 8 (OR) — Algorithmic pricing and tacit collusion

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
8Evaluate claim about inevitable tacit collusion:
  • Definition: algorithmic / dynamic pricing uses real-time data; reinforcement-learning algorithms may converge on supra-competitive prices without explicit agreement.
  • Theoretical basis: Calvano et al. (2020) experiments show pricing algorithms can reach collusive outcomes autonomously.
  • Real-world evidence: fuel retail (Germany), online retail (Amazon Marketplace), airline yield-management.
  • Counter: dynamic pricing often procompetitive — improves allocative efficiency, clears inventory.
  • Consumer benefit from personalised discounts; price discrimination can expand output.
  • Detection challenge: legal test for collusion traditionally requires evidence of concerted practice; algorithmic convergence without communication is a new regulatory challenge.
  • 'Prohibited practice' approach: blanket ban likely over-broad; would prohibit legitimate procompetitive pricing.
  • Alternative: transparency and auditability requirements; duty to test for anti-competitive outcomes.
  • 'Inevitably' is too strong: depends on market structure, price observability, frequency of interaction.
  • Judgement: targeted regulation (monitoring + disclosure) preferable to prohibition.

Question 6(b) – Discuss the likely impact of the government's proposed "build-out" penalty on a major house-builder's profitability (12 marks)

Diagram required: cost/revenue diagram showing two scenarios (status quo vs. with penalty).

KAA with diagram (out of 8)
  • Scenario 1 (status quo): firm builds slowly, high margins, supernormal profit area large.
  • Scenario 2 (with penalty): firm forced to accelerate build — accelerated build shifts supply right, reducing market P; also increases MC short-run (labour/materials shortages).
  • Show profit-maximising output shifting and supernormal profit area shrinking.
  • Barratt Redrow: 85,000 plots at 17,200 p.a. = 4.9 years, within proposed 3-year limit — but Bellway at 12.7 years would face material penalty costs.
  • Estimated penalty cost if builder can't accelerate: potentially £hundreds of millions.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Penalty only bites if site is genuinely capable of faster build-out.
  • Small sites can accelerate; large strategic sites (1,000+ homes) genuinely require 5–10 year phased construction.
  • Risk that penalty raises land-acquisition risk → reduces future supply (perverse effect highlighted by HBF).
  • Market reaction: builders may offload slow-build land and concentrate on fast-turn sites.
  • Judgement: effect on individual firm profitability is ambiguous — depends on land-bank composition, ability to accelerate, and whether penalty causes market-wide supply response that depresses prices.

Question 6(c) – Examine two factors that may explain why UK house-builders hold large consented land banks (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Planning-system uncertainty (Extract A): 4.5-year average from acquisition to consent; without pipeline, build rates collapse. Land banks act as insurance against planning risk.
  • Operational continuity: £2–4m sunk costs per site; maintaining skilled labour and supplier relationships requires forward visibility of work.
  • Strategic / profit motive: land banks enable output restriction if market prices fall; “trickle-out” sustains margins on large sites.
  • Land as speculative asset: consented land rises in value; some land banking reflects portfolio management, not production planning.
  • Industry structure: oligopolistic market with interdependence — rival builders matching “slow build-out” can be a Nash equilibrium.
  • Evaluation reach: distinction between defensive (planning insurance) and offensive (price-support) motives matters for policy — the CMA's findings suggest both are present.

Award 8-mark "Examine" band (KAA = 5, Eval = 3).

Question 6(d) – Assess whether the behaviour of UK house-builders is consistent with collusive oligopoly (10 marks)

KAA (out of 6)

Evidence FOR collusive behaviour:

  • CMA 2024 findings: information-sharing at industry events; £60m fines.
  • Persistent land-bank years-of-supply 7–18 years — suggests supply restriction.
  • Parallel build-out rates across firms (tacit coordination).
  • Oligopoly concentration at top (CR3 among top 10 = 57.6%).
  • Stable margins despite demand volatility.

Evidence AGAINST pure collusion:

  • Planning system is a genuine binding constraint — land banks partly defensive.
  • HBF argues output constraints are regulatory, not strategic.
  • Entry of modular/volumetric builders (e.g. Vistry's partnership model).
  • Price competition at local level via incentives (stamp duty paid, carpets included).
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Pattern of behaviour is consistent with tacit collusion but not definitively proven.
  • CMA's finding falls short of full cartel prosecution.
  • Parallel slow build-out could reflect rational response to planning risk OR tacit coordination — hard to separate empirically.
  • Vistry's entry with partnership model (building for housing associations and affordable providers) shows industry is not perfectly rigid.
  • Judgement: behaviour shows features of tacit collusion (parallel slow build-out, information sharing) mixed with genuine regulatory friction. “Quasi-collusive oligopoly with planning-system cover” is the most defensible characterisation.

Question 6(e) – Discuss whether behavioural economics provides a better framework than traditional market failure analysis for understanding the UK housing market (15 marks)

KAA (out of 9)

Traditional market failure framework:

  • Externalities (infrastructure pressure, biodiversity, congestion).
  • Information asymmetry (NHBC warranty as imperfect fix).
  • Market power (oligopolistic house-builders, planning-constrained land market).
  • Merit/public goods (social housing, affordable housing provision).

Behavioural framework (from Extract B):

  • Anchoring on past prices — 31% refuse to list below purchase price.
  • Loss aversion — homeowners hold on to losing properties, slowing market adjustment.
  • Limited attention — developers exploit complex pricing bundles.
  • Status quo bias — homeowners under-invest in energy efficiency.
  • Framing effects — £349,995 vs. £350,000.
Evaluation (out of 6)
  • Behavioural framework captures pricing stickiness and consumer exploitation that traditional models miss.
  • Traditional framework better captures supply-side structural problems (planning, land banking, oligopoly).
  • Policy implications differ: behavioural → nudges, mandated disclosures, cooling-off periods; traditional → planning reform, competition enforcement, tax incentives.
  • Both frameworks complementary rather than substitutable.
  • Judgement: neither framework alone is sufficient. Behavioural economics illuminates the demand side (especially consumer protection), while traditional market failure captures the supply side (planning, market structure). A “best-of-both” policy mix is likely optimal. Pure behavioural focus misses structural supply constraints; pure market-failure focus misses consumer exploitation and sticky adjustment.

Section C — Question 7 OR Question 8 (25 marks)

Question 7 – Evaluate whether firms with market power should be permitted to use price discrimination (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Price discrimination theory: 1st / 2nd / 3rd degree; conditions (market power, separable markets, differing PED, no arbitrage).
  • Welfare effects: can increase output and access (1st-degree captures all CS); can reduce CS; allocative effects ambiguous.
  • Diagrams: 3rd-degree PD diagram showing higher P to inelastic group, lower P to elastic group; total output may rise.
  • Application (supermarket dynamic pricing, airline yield management, rail peak/off-peak): consumers differ in willingness-to-pay; dynamic pricing can reduce food waste.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Efficiency case: PD can expand output (esp. for high fixed-cost firms — airlines, pharmaceuticals).
  • Consumer harm: 1st-degree PD transfers all surplus to firm; algorithmic PD risks discrimination against vulnerable consumers.
  • Fairness/equity: disparate impact on low-income consumers if they are in inelastic segment.
  • Dynamic effects: PD funds R&D investment (pharma case).
  • Regulatory perspective: CMA focus on algorithmic transparency; potential for “data-driven” discrimination based on protected characteristics.
  • Judgement: PD should generally be permitted where it expands output and where consumers are not systematically exploited based on vulnerability. Regulatory guardrails (transparency, no protected-characteristic discrimination, cooling-off for high-value purchases) address the worst abuses without blocking efficiency gains.

Question 8 – Evaluate the case for government intervention to correct market failure in a specific market of your choice (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Market failure types: externalities, public goods, merit/demerit, information asymmetry, market power, inequality.
  • Intervention tools: Pigouvian taxes/subsidies, regulation, direct provision, tradable permits, information provision, competition policy.
  • Government failure: regulatory capture, unintended consequences, time inconsistency.
  • Application (higher education, climate policy, pharmaceuticals, childcare): specific evidence and policy history.
  • Diagrams: externality diagram with MSC, MSB; public good / merit good diagrams.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Intervention justified where market failure is clearly identified and government can plausibly do better.
  • Risk of government failure: imperfect information, political capture, regulatory lag.
  • Empirical evidence: mixed across interventions (congestion charges effective, rent controls often counter-productive).
  • Second-best theory: partial interventions in the presence of multiple market failures can make things worse.
  • Distributional concerns: interventions often have regressive or unintended equity effects.
  • Judgement: intervention is justified where market failure is material, where the chosen instrument is well-matched to the failure, and where government has credible implementation capacity. Blanket “intervene vs. don't intervene” framing is unhelpful; design quality is the binding constraint.

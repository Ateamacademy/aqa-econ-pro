General Marking Guidance

All candidates must receive the same treatment. Examiners must mark the first candidate in exactly the same way as they mark the last. Mark schemes should be applied positively. Candidates must be rewarded for what they have shown they can do rather than penalised for omissions.

Crossed-out work should be marked unless the candidate has replaced it with an alternative response. All the marks on the mark scheme are designed to be awarded. Examiners should always award full marks if deserved, i.e. if the answer matches the mark scheme.

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • (a) Price discrimination = charging different prices to different consumers for identical products with no cost justification.
  • Clubcard Prices meets the conditions: identifiable consumer groups (members vs non-members); no arbitrage; market power.
  • Represents second-/third-degree discrimination depending on whether it captures willingness-to-pay segmentation.
Answer: B
Second-degree = prices vary with quantity or version purchased (e.g. loyalty tiers, bulk discounts).

Question 3 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Rise of discounters shifts demand for branded goods left (D to D₂).
  • Equilibrium price falls (P₁ to P₂); quantity falls (Q₁ to Q₂).
  • Consumer surplus rises for private-label buyers; branded producers face lower margins.
  • Producer surplus on brands falls; industry concentration falls as discounters gain share.
Answer: B
Falling costs of small-format operations and private-label sourcing enable the discount model to scale.

Question 4 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Oligopoly cost-revenue diagram: AR and MR with MC and AC.
  • Price cut reduces P and hence AR; with unchanged costs, supernormal profit rectangle shrinks.
  • Kinked-demand hypothesis: if rivals match the cut, the demand curve is relatively inelastic below the kink, compounding margin loss.
Answer: A
Predatory pricing (pricing below cost to drive out competitors) is illegal under Chapter II of Competition Act 1998.

Question 5 — Total 5 marks

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Rising wages increase supermarket costs → squeeze margins or pass through to prices.
  • Demand effects: higher incomes for low-paid workers raise demand for groceries.
  • Employment: may reduce hours or increase automation (self-checkouts).
Answer: B
Price-elastic labour demand means firms substitute away from workers when labour costs rise → bigger employment fall.

Extract A
Generative AI and the task-based model of the labour market

Economic research on the labour-market effects of information technology has increasingly adopted a task-based framework. Occupations are decomposed into bundles of tasks — routine cognitive, non-routine cognitive, routine manual, and non-routine manual — each of which differs in the ease with which it can be performed by machines.

Earlier waves of digital technology automated routine tasks, generating the well-documented 'hollowing out' of the wage distribution: declining employment and wages for mid-skilled routine-task workers, combined with growing demand for both low-skilled non-routine service workers and high-skilled non-routine cognitive workers.

Generative AI differs from earlier information technologies in important respects. Evidence suggests that it substitutes for some non-routine cognitive tasks previously thought to be the preserve of high-skilled professionals, particularly in writing, coding, analysis and design. Early productivity studies report gains ranging from 14% to 37% for workers performing these tasks with AI augmentation, with the largest gains concentrated among less-experienced workers.

Economists disagree sharply about the aggregate implications. Optimists argue AI represents a general-purpose technology that will raise total-factor productivity across the economy once complementary organisational changes occur. Pessimists warn of a renewed productivity paradox in which visible adoption fails to translate into measured aggregate productivity growth, and of a concentration of gains in the hands of the firms that own proprietary models and compute infrastructure.

(Source: adapted from OECD Employment Outlook and the Brookings Institution, 2025)

Extract B
The Mundell–Fleming trilemma and the global financial cycle

The classic Mundell–Fleming trilemma (or 'impossible trinity') states that an open economy cannot simultaneously maintain a fixed exchange rate, free capital mobility and independent monetary policy: at most two of the three are achievable at any time.

Recent empirical research, most associated with Hélène Rey of London Business School, has argued that the trilemma has in practice collapsed into a dilemma: even countries with floating exchange rates find their domestic financial conditions substantially driven by a global financial cycle in gross capital flows, credit growth and asset prices, the dynamics of which are closely linked to US monetary policy.

The implication is that exchange-rate flexibility alone does not deliver full monetary-policy independence, particularly for small open economies with developed capital markets. Preserving independence, Rey argues, requires direct measures to dampen capital-flow volatility: targeted macroprudential regulation, and in some cases explicit capital controls — a position once regarded as professionally taboo, now increasingly accepted by the IMF itself through its Institutional View on capital-flow management.

The 2008–09 global financial crisis, the 2013 'taper tantrum', and the 2022–23 US monetary tightening episode all generated substantial emerging-market capital-flow reversals, sharp exchange-rate depreciations, and macroeconomic stress disproportionate to the domestic fundamentals of the affected economies.

(Source: adapted from Rey, H. 'Dilemma not Trilemma', IMF policy papers and BIS research, 2024)

The US-China decoupling

Between 2000 and 2015 the US and China became deeply economically interdependent. Chinese exports rose from 3.9% of global goods trade in 2000 to 14.1% by 2015, while US manufacturing employment fell by around 5.7 million jobs over the same period. This period of "hyperglobalisation" has since given way to a policy-driven decoupling.

Since 2018 the US has imposed successive rounds of tariffs on Chinese imports, culminating in blanket tariffs on all Chinese goods in 2025. Export controls have targeted advanced semiconductors, biotech, and quantum computing. The CHIPS Act and the Inflation Reduction Act offer subsidies to re-shore strategic manufacturing. China has responded with its own restrictions on rare earths exports and subsidies to domestic technology champions.

Economists are divided on the welfare effects. Supporters of decoupling argue it addresses genuine national-security risks and corrects for Chinese industrial policy and IP theft. Critics cite evidence that the tariffs have been passed through largely to US consumers (around 90% of the burden according to Autor et al.), that the re-shoring gains are modest, and that the fragmentation of supply chains threatens a repeat of 1930s-style protectionism.

(Source: adapted from Peterson Institute & The Economist, 2025)

The dollar hegemony debate & CBDCs

The US dollar's position as the world's dominant reserve and invoicing currency has given the US what former French finance minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing called an "exorbitant privilege": seigniorage revenue, lower borrowing costs, the ability to apply financial sanctions extraterritorially, and enhanced monetary autonomy. These advantages are estimated to reduce US long-term interest rates by around 40–80 basis points.

Since 2022 the weaponisation of the dollar payments system through sanctions on Russia has accelerated efforts to develop alternatives. China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) has grown rapidly, and the BRICS grouping has discussed a common reserve currency. Most significantly, over 100 central banks are now exploring Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) in part to reduce dependence on US-controlled infrastructure. China's digital yuan (e-CNY) has reached over 260 million users.

Sceptics argue that none of these alternatives seriously threatens the dollar's dominance, because the dollar's role rests on the scale, liquidity, and rule-of-law protections of US capital markets. Others argue that fragmentation is proceeding more rapidly than the headline reserve-share numbers suggest, through the growth of bilateral invoicing arrangements and non-dollar trade settlement.

(Source: adapted from Council on Foreign Relations & BIS, 2025)

Extract A
Hyperscalers, market power and AI

The global market for cloud infrastructure is dominated by three “hyperscalers”: AWS, Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud. Together they hold 65% of the market in Q4 2024. Barriers to entry are very high: a single hyperscale data centre costs \$1–2 billion to build, and operating advanced silicon (GPUs and custom AI chips) requires scale that smaller firms cannot match.

The AI boom has concentrated demand. NVIDIA’s GPUs – essential for training large language models – are sold predominantly to hyperscalers and a few AI labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, Meta). NVIDIA held an estimated 80% share of the data-centre AI accelerator market in 2024, with gross margins exceeding 60%.

Microsoft’s strategic partnership with OpenAI (around \$13 billion invested since 2019) gives it privileged access to ChatGPT and GPT-4 through Azure. Google and Amazon have responded with their own model families (Gemini and Claude-Bedrock partnerships) and are investing over \$100 billion combined per year on data-centre capacity.

Regulators are increasingly concerned. The UK’s CMA opened a market investigation into cloud services in 2024, citing “egress fees”, technical interoperability barriers and committed-spend discount structures that allegedly lock customers in.

(Source: adapted from https://www.gov.uk/cma-cloud-services-market-investigation)

Extract B
Productivity, wages and the “AI wage premium”

In 2024 US software-engineer salaries in AI-adjacent roles rose 18% year-on-year, while median software-engineer salaries rose just 3%. In the UK, average wages in Information and Communication grew 7.4% in 2024 compared with 4.9% for the whole economy. Firms competing for a limited pool of ML engineers offered signing bonuses exceeding \$1 million for senior hires.

At the other end of the labour market, AI-driven automation is beginning to displace routine-cognitive workers. A 2024 IMF study estimated that around 40% of jobs globally are “exposed” to AI, of which roughly half face substantial substitution risk; in developed economies the substitution share rises to 60% of exposed jobs.

(Source: adapted from https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/Staff-Discussion-Notes/2024/AI-Labour-Markets)

Extract C
The macro context

The scale of AI-related investment is reshaping aggregate demand. Goldman Sachs estimates global spending on AI data centres and associated infrastructure will reach \$1 trillion by 2028, up from \$200 billion in 2023. In 2024 alone, this investment contributed an estimated 0.3–0.5 percentage points to US real GDP growth. However, this investment is extraordinarily concentrated: the top five hyperscalers and AI labs account for over 60% of capital spending. Some economists compare the current phase to the dot-com boom of 1998–2000, warning of asset-price risk if AI monetisation fails.

(Source: adapted from https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/artificial-intelligence-economic-outlook)

Extract D
The UK’s productivity puzzle

UK productivity growth has averaged just 0.5% per year since 2008, compared with a pre-crisis trend of around 2% and around 1% for peer economies. By 2024, UK output per hour was around 15–20% below the level it would have reached had pre-2008 trends continued. Business investment as a share of GDP has been persistently lower than in France, Germany or the US.

Analysts cite multiple overlapping causes: weak capital deepening, poor diffusion of best-practice management and technology, Brexit-related trade frictions, planning-system delays, and a skills gap in digital and technical roles.

The 2024 UK Budget introduced “full expensing” of qualifying plant and machinery investment. The OBR estimates full expensing could raise business investment by 3% in steady state. Critics argue the policy is expensive (around £10 billion in lost corporation tax per year) and that the binding UK constraint is demand, management quality and planning – not the after-tax cost of capital.

(Source: adapted from https://www.resolutionfoundation.org/publications/productivity-puzzle)

Extract E
Labour market tightness and wage pressures

The UK labour market remains tight relative to historical norms. In mid-2025, unemployment was 4.5% and vacancies remained elevated at around 720,000. Economic inactivity – particularly among 50–64-year-olds and due to long-term sickness – has remained above pre-pandemic levels.

Nominal wage growth averaged 5.3% in the year to Q2 2025, driven by both labour scarcity and the lagged effect of catching up with the 2022–23 inflation shock. The Bank of England has warned services inflation (around 4.5%) is being kept elevated by these wage dynamics.

The government’s supply-side package combines full expensing, apprenticeship levy reform, planning reform for major infrastructure, and targeted skills bootcamps. The Treasury hopes these measures will raise trend growth from 1.3% to 1.7% over a decade.

(Source: adapted from https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report)

Extract A
The discounter revolution

Aldi and Lidl have transformed the UK grocery market over the past decade. Both operate a limited-range, high-volume model with around 2,000 SKUs per store compared to 30,000+ in a Tesco Extra. Their emphasis on low prices, private-label products and efficient logistics has enabled them to undercut the Big 4 on headline prices by 15–20%.

Between 2015 and 2025 Aldi's UK market share rose from 5.6% to 11.2%, overtaking Morrisons to become the UK's fourth-largest grocer. The incumbents have responded with 'price-match' campaigns, loyalty-card discounts, and own-label value ranges. Industry analysts describe the result as a prolonged period of grocery price compression, interrupted only by the 2022–23 food-inflation shock.

(Source: adapted from The Grocer, 2025)

Extract B
Loyalty-card scrutiny

In 2024 the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) launched an investigation into supermarket loyalty-card pricing, responding to consumer concerns that some 'Clubcard' and 'Nectar' prices may not represent genuine savings. The CMA's interim report noted that while loyalty schemes can deliver value for engaged shoppers, they may disadvantage less-digitally-engaged consumers including older customers and low-income households.

Retailers argue that loyalty schemes are essential for managing customer retention in a market where switching costs are low and the discounters pose a credible outside option.

(Source: adapted from CMA report, 2024)

Extract C
CMA grocery sector review

In early 2025 the CMA launched a comprehensive market study of the UK grocery sector, the first since 2008. The review will examine pricing practices, supplier relationships, planning restrictions on new store openings, and the interaction between physical and online grocery markets.

Supermarket CEOs argue that the sector is already one of the most competitive in the UK economy, citing the rapid gains by discounters as evidence. Consumer groups and the Food Foundation counter that inflation-adjusted margins remain above pre-pandemic levels, and that vulnerable consumers face a 'poverty premium' through inability to access loyalty discounts.

(Source: adapted from Financial Times, 2025)

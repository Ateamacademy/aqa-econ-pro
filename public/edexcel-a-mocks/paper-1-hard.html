Extract A
Concentration and switching inertia in UK retail banking

The UK's retail banking market has long been characterised by a small number of incumbent firms controlling the majority of personal current accounts. Despite the launch of the Current Account Switch Service in 2013, annual switching rates have hovered around 1.5% — well below levels seen in comparable consumer markets.

The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) has argued that high search costs, consumer inertia and the complexity of banking products together reduce the competitive pressure that would otherwise discipline incumbent banks on price and service quality. Bank profit margins on personal accounts remained among the highest in the OECD through 2024.

Behavioural economists point additionally to loss aversion: consumers disproportionately weight the perceived risk of switching errors (such as missed payments) against the potential gains from lower fees or better interest rates.

(Source: adapted from the CMA Retail Banking Market Study update, 2024)

Extract B
Challenger banks and open banking

A wave of 'challenger banks' — including Monzo, Starling and Revolut — has emerged over the past decade, reaching a combined 16% market share in personal current accounts by 2024. Their cost base is structurally lower than the incumbents, reflecting the absence of branch networks and legacy IT systems.

Open banking, introduced under the CMA's 2017 retail-banking remedies, requires the largest banks to share customer data with authorised third parties via secure APIs. Proponents argue this dramatically reduces search costs and enables innovative products such as account-aggregation apps, smart-savings tools and instant affordability checks.

Critics observe, however, that incumbents have responded by acquiring promising fintech entrants, raising concerns that initial competitive benefits could be eroded over time.

(Source: adapted from Financial Times fintech coverage, 2024)

Extract C
A proposed tie-up of two challenger banks

In 2025 two leading challenger banks announced plans for an all-share merger. If approved, the combined entity would become the fifth-largest UK retail bank by deposits and the second-largest by active digital users.

Supporters highlight economies of scope in technology investment and the ability to compete more effectively with the 'Big Four' on mortgages and business banking. Opponents argue the merger would reduce the number of serious rivals to the incumbents and note that both banks have relied heavily on aggressive introductory pricing to acquire customers.

(Source: adapted from The Times business section, 2025)

Extract A
Land banking under scrutiny

UK house-builders hold sufficient land with planning permission to build well over 1 million homes — a multi-year pipeline. Critics argue that house-builders deliberately restrict output to support prices, pointing to the fact that the eight largest builders consistently build out their sites over 5–15 years rather than in 1–3 years. The CMA's 2024 market study into housebuilding found “anti-competitive behaviour by some firms” including information-sharing at industry events, and issued a £60 million fine. It also found that the planning system itself is the binding constraint in many local markets.

Major builders have argued that land banks are necessary because the planning system is slow and unpredictable. Average time from land acquisition to planning consent is 4.5 years. Without multi-year land pipelines, builders argue, they cannot maintain stable workforces or supplier relationships. The sunk costs of establishing a site are estimated at £2–4 million.

The UK government's 2024 proposal to require builders to “build out” consented sites within three years or face penalties was welcomed by housing charities but opposed by the Home Builders Federation, which warned it would reduce, not increase, output by making land acquisition riskier.

(Source: adapted from https://www.gov.uk/cma-housebuilding-market-study-2024)

Extract B
Behavioural economics and the housing market

Housing markets exhibit significant departures from rational-agent assumptions. Buyers exhibit anchoring on historic prices and loss aversion — 2024 research found 31% of UK homeowners whose homes had fallen below purchase price refused to list at market price, preferring to delay sale. Developers exploit limited consumer attention: showroom pricing often uses £-just-below-round-number prices (e.g. £349,995) and incentive structures bundle white goods, carpets, and stamp duty into mortgage — disguising true cost.

Information asymmetries are severe: buyers rarely know build quality until defects emerge post-completion. The National House Building Council (NHBC) warranty system partly addresses this but is insurance-based rather than a quality guarantee. Critics argue that the warranty distributes losses rather than creating incentives for quality construction.

(Source: adapted from https://www.resolutionfoundation.org/publications/housing-market-behavioural-economics-2024)

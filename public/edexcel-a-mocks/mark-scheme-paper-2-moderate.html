General Marking Guidance

All candidates must receive the same treatment. Examiners must mark the last candidate in exactly the same way as they mark the first.

Examiners should mark according to the mark scheme – not according to their perception of where the grade boundaries may lie.

All the marks on the mark scheme are designed to be awarded. Examiners should always award full marks if deserved; that is, if the answer matches the mark scheme.

Where some judgement is required, mark schemes will provide the principles by which marks will be awarded and exemplification may be limited.

When examiners are in doubt regarding the application of the mark scheme to a candidate's response, the team leader must be consulted.

Crossed-out work should be marked UNLESS the candidate has replaced it with an alternative response.

Question 6(b) – Examine two factors that may explain the slowdown in UK GDP growth shown in Figure 2 (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Monetary tightening: Bank Rate rose from 0.1% (Dec 2021) to 5.25% (Aug 2023), dampening consumption and investment through higher borrowing costs.
  • Cost-of-living crisis: CPI peaked at 11.1% in Oct 2022, eroding real incomes and consumption.
  • Weak investment: UK business investment has grown slower than peers since 2016; Brexit uncertainty and planning constraints restrict capacity.
  • External demand: Eurozone growth weak in 2024; the UK's main trading partner.
  • Fiscal tightening: debt interest absorbed £105bn in 2024–25, crowding out discretionary spending.
  • Productivity: UK productivity growth has lagged peers since 2008, constraining trend growth.

Award 8-mark "Examine" band (KAA = 5, Eval = 3).

Question 6(c) – Assess the likely impact of a persistent current account deficit on the UK economy (10 marks)

KAA (out of 6)
  • 3.5% of GDP deficit is sizeable by OECD standards.
  • Current account = trade balance + income balance + transfers.
  • Sterling depreciation could narrow deficit via Marshall-Lerner (if PED sum > 1).
  • Financing through capital account (FDI, portfolio inflows, reserves).
  • Services surplus (finance, education, digital) partially offsets goods deficit.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Deficit sustainable if financed by long-term FDI; unsustainable if dependent on short-term “hot money”.
  • Can indicate strong domestic demand (positive) or weak export competitiveness (negative).
  • Sterling is a reserve-status currency with deep markets — financing less of a constraint than for EM economies.
  • Persistent deficit requires persistent capital inflow; any sudden stop causes currency crisis.
  • Judgement: manageable in short-run given sterling's status and services surplus; worrying if goods deficit reflects structural competitiveness decline.

Question 6(d) – Discuss the likely success of the Bank of England's interest rate cuts in raising UK aggregate demand (12 marks)

Diagram required (typically AD/AS showing AD shift right).

KAA with diagram (out of 8)
  • AD/AS model: rate cut → lower cost of borrowing → ↑C and ↑I → AD shifts right → ↑real GDP, ↑price level.
  • Transmission channels: borrowing costs, exchange rate (depreciation boosts X), asset prices (wealth effect), consumer confidence.
  • Time lags: monetary policy effect takes 12–24 months.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Effectiveness depends on consumer and business confidence, not just rates.
  • High household debt means debt-servicing relief boosts spending immediately for some.
  • Services inflation sticky at 4–5% could anchor high inflation expectations, reducing real impact.
  • Liquidity trap not an issue (rates above zero lower bound).
  • Supply-side constraints limit how much extra AD becomes higher real output vs. higher prices.
  • Judgement: rate cuts will boost AD somewhat but may be insufficient to return growth to trend without complementary fiscal or supply-side measures.

Question 6(e) – Discuss whether supply-side policies (Extract A) are likely to raise UK long-run growth (15 marks)

KAA (out of 9)
  • Supply-side policies described: full-expensing investment allowance; planning reform; apprenticeship levy expansion.
  • LRAS framework: supply-side policies aim to shift LRAS right through capital deepening, skills, infrastructure.
  • Full expensing reduces effective cost of capital by up to 25% → higher firm-level investment.
  • Planning reform lowers transaction costs and time-to-build for infrastructure and housing.
  • Apprenticeship reform targets labour-market friction and skills mismatch.
  • Expected effect: 0.2–0.4 percentage points added to trend growth over a decade (IFS estimate).
Evaluation (out of 6)
  • Supply-side effects take 5–10 years to materialise.
  • Full expensing: evidence mixed internationally; works when firms are capital-constrained AND demand is adequate.
  • Planning reform: politically fragile; change in government could reverse.
  • Deeper UK structural issues (R&D < 3% of GDP, weak management, regional imbalance) beyond scope of package.
  • Interaction with macro conditions: supply-side works only if demand is adequate.
  • Judgement: package is directionally correct and, if sustained, could close ~half the gap between current trend growth and pre-2008 levels; execution risk is the binding constraint, not design.

Section C — Question 7 OR Question 8 (25 marks)

Question 7 – Evaluate the likely effects of an increase in protectionism on the macroeconomy of a developed country of your choice (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Tariff theory: increase in price of imports; domestic price rises; welfare analysis (tariff diagram: DWL areas, producer surplus gain, tariff revenue rectangle).
  • AD effect: protectionism raises X−M in short run by reducing M, raising AD.
  • AS effect: higher input costs (if imports are intermediate goods) shift SRAS left.
  • Inflation: tariffs directly raise CPI via imported inflation.
  • Retaliation: exports from home country fall, reducing AD.
  • Global growth: WTO/IMF estimate tariff wars can reduce world GDP 0.5–1%.
  • Application to named country (e.g. USA): 2024–25 tariff rounds added 0.4pp to US CPI.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Depends on size of protectionism, targeted sector, and retaliation.
  • Short-term employment protection in declining sectors vs. long-term productivity cost.
  • Strategic argument: protection of critical supply chains (semiconductors, medicines) may be welfare-improving.
  • Exchange rate offset: sterling/dollar may appreciate, offsetting tariff benefits to exporters.
  • Consumer welfare costs typically outweigh producer gains (classical welfare analysis).
  • Judgement: protectionism generally reduces aggregate welfare in a developed economy, though strategic exceptions exist; case-by-case policy design matters more than blanket protection.

Question 8 – Evaluate the likely macroeconomic consequences of a significant rise in global oil prices for an oil-importing developed country (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Cost-push inflation: oil is a key input; SRAS shifts left.
  • AD/AS diagram: SRAS leftward shift → higher P, lower real GDP (stagflation risk).
  • Balance of payments: import bill rises → wider current-account deficit.
  • Exchange rate: current-account deficit widening → potential depreciation → further imported inflation.
  • Consumer real income: higher fuel prices squeeze discretionary spending → AD falls.
  • Monetary policy: central bank faces dilemma — raise rates to fight inflation or cut to support growth.
  • Fiscal: government may subsidise fuel (costly) or accept distributional damage.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Magnitude depends on oil intensity of GDP — developed economies less exposed than in 1970s due to services shift.
  • UK North Sea production softens net impact — partially self-sufficient.
  • Transmission speed: petrol/diesel fastest; electricity depends on fuel mix.
  • Inflation expectations: anchored expectations reduce wage-price spiral risk.
  • Substitution: renewables investment may accelerate, reducing long-run dependence.
  • Judgement: significant oil price rise causes short-run stagflation in oil-importing developed economies but magnitude is smaller than historical precedent. Monetary policy faces a difficult trade-off; supply-side response (accelerating renewables, energy efficiency) is the durable answer.

Extract A
The “Big Six” and competition in UK retail energy

The UK domestic energy retail market was long dominated by the “Big Six”: Centrica (British Gas), E.ON, SSE, EDF, ScottishPower, and RWE npower. Between 2010 and 2019 over 70 smaller challenger suppliers entered the market, promising lower prices and better service. However, the 2021–22 wholesale gas price spike – triggered by the Russian invasion of Ukraine – caused 29 suppliers to go out of business. Ofgem, the energy regulator, was criticised for permitting too many under-capitalised entrants.

By 2024 the market had re-consolidated: British Gas, Octopus Energy, EDF, E.ON Next, Scottish Power, and OVO collectively served over 85% of British households. Octopus Energy, using technology-led pricing and customer service, had grown from a 2016 start-up to the UK’s largest supplier by 2024, with over 25% market share.

The Ofgem price cap, introduced in 2019, adjusts quarterly based on wholesale costs and is binding on standard variable tariffs. Competition regulators note that while the cap protected consumers during the crisis, it also constrains price competition below the cap, potentially reducing incentives to innovate.

(Source: adapted from https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/energy-data-and-research)

Extract B
Emissions, externalities and the carbon price

Electricity generation is the UK’s second-largest source of greenhouse gas emissions. Since 2013 the UK has operated a “Carbon Price Support” on top of the UK ETS. Carbon pricing has been credited with driving the near-elimination of coal from the UK generation mix – coal’s share fell from 22.5% in 2015 to under 1% in 2024.

However, critics argue carbon pricing alone does not account for all external costs. Wind and solar installations impose visual externalities on local communities; nuclear generates long-lived radioactive waste; biomass combustion releases CO₂ that may not be offset by new growth within policy-relevant timescales. In 2024 the UK carbon price averaged £42 per tonne, down from a 2022 peak of £98.

(Source: adapted from https://www.theccc.org.uk/publication/progress-in-reducing-emissions)

Extract C
Inflation, wages and energy

The 2022 wholesale gas price surge contributed decisively to the UK’s cost-of-living crisis. Domestic energy CPI rose 89% year-on-year at its peak in October 2022. Headline CPI inflation peaked at 11.1% that month. By mid-2024 energy CPI had fallen back sharply as wholesale prices normalised, but the episode left a lasting imprint on inflation expectations and real wages.

Real wages in the UK had only just returned to their pre-pandemic level by the end of 2024. Services inflation – which tracks domestically produced services – remained stubborn at around 4–5% through most of 2024, reflecting earlier wage catch-up after the real-income shock.

(Source: adapted from https://www.ons.gov.uk/economy/inflationandpriceindices)

Extract D
The Bank of England’s tightening and easing cycle

The Bank of England’s Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) raised the Bank Rate from 0.1% in December 2021 to a peak of 5.25% in August 2023 – the steepest tightening cycle in more than three decades. By mid-2024 CPI had fallen back to the 2% target, and the MPC began cutting rates in a cautious cycle, reaching 4.50% by early 2025.

Transmission of rate changes in the UK has become less predictable since 2022. Over 85% of UK mortgages are fixed-rate, typically for 2–5 years, so higher rates affect household disposable incomes with long lags. The Treasury estimates that around 1.5 million households re-fixed their mortgages in 2024, with an average monthly payment increase of £240.

Business investment remained weak through 2023–2024, reflecting a combination of Brexit-related trade frictions, planning delays, and weak external demand. The OBR estimates the UK’s potential growth rate has fallen to around 1.3–1.5% – well below pre-2008 rates.

(Source: adapted from https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report)

Extract E
Supply-side reforms and the UK’s growth challenge

The UK Chancellor announced a package of supply-side measures in the October 2024 Budget: full-expensing for qualifying plant and machinery investment, a £15 billion housing programme targeted at brownfield and grey-belt sites, expansion of the apprenticeship levy, reform of the planning system for large infrastructure, and a new investment zone framework.

Economists at the Institute for Fiscal Studies welcomed the planning reform as “potentially the most significant pro-growth measure in a decade” but noted that “much depends on execution.” They argued the measures could raise trend growth by 0.2–0.4 percentage points over ten years if fully implemented, but that the UK’s productivity gap reflects structural issues – R&D investment below 3% of GDP, infrastructure quality ranked outside G7 top-half, weak management practices – that no single package can address.

(Source: adapted from https://www.ifs.org.uk/publications/budget-analysis-2024)

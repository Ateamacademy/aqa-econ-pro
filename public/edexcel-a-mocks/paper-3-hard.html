Extract A
The incumbent–challenger dynamic in UK retail banking

The UK personal current account market was historically dominated by the “Big Four” (Lloyds, Barclays, NatWest, HSBC), which together held around 70% of all accounts in 2010. Since 2015, digital-first challengers – Monzo, Starling, and Revolut – have grown aggressively. By 2024 Monzo alone was opening 20.7% of all new UK personal current accounts. Yet in total existing account stocks, the Big Four still held over 60% – the inertia of established accounts is a major competitive barrier.

Challenger banks compete on app quality, speed of onboarding, fee transparency and customer service. Their operating model – no branches, cloud-native infrastructure – allows cost-to-serve of around £30 per customer per year, compared with £150+ at the Big Four.

The CMA has required Open Banking since 2018, intended to reduce switching costs by allowing portable financial data. Uptake has been slower than hoped. CMA research finds that while most new-account customers at challenger banks are aware of Open Banking, only 12% of Big Four customers have used any Open Banking service.

(Source: adapted from https://www.gov.uk/cma-retail-banking-market-investigation-progress)

Extract B
Capital requirements and competition

Since the 2008 global financial crisis, UK banks have faced progressively tighter capital requirements. Under Basel III, banks must hold Common Equity Tier 1 capital of at least 4.5% of risk-weighted assets, plus additional buffers totalling 10%+ for systemically important banks. The 2024 “Basel 3.1” framework raises these requirements further.

Critics argue capital regulation imposes disproportionate fixed costs of compliance that favour larger, established banks. Monzo’s 2024 accounts noted £47 million in regulatory compliance spend – material for a firm with £880 million of revenue. This is cited as a reason for consolidation (Virgin Money’s acquisition by Nationwide in 2024) and as an entry barrier.

Proponents argue bank failures have catastrophic externalities (2008, Silicon Valley Bank 2023, Credit Suisse 2023). The social marginal cost of bank failure greatly exceeds the private marginal cost; capital requirements are therefore a Pigouvian-style instrument to internalise systemic externalities.

(Source: adapted from https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/paper/2024/basel-3-1-near-final-policy)

Extract C
Monetary policy, bank profitability and transmission

Higher interest rates since 2022 have dramatically improved UK bank profitability. Net interest margin – the gap between what banks earn on lending and pay on deposits – rose from 1.5% in 2021 to 3.1% in 2024. UK Big Four banks collectively reported £40 billion in profits in 2024, the highest level in nominal terms since before 2008.

The MPC has warned that monetary policy transmission to UK households has been slower than in previous cycles, partly because 85%+ of UK mortgages are fixed-rate for 2–5 years. In 2024, approximately 1.5 million households rolled onto new fixed rates, with an average monthly payment rise of £240.

(Source: adapted from https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report)

Extract D
The gilt-yield surge and debt dynamics

The September 2022 “mini-budget” crisis was a vivid illustration of modern debt dynamics. Unfunded tax-cut announcements triggered a sudden rise in gilt yields from 3.0% to over 4.5% within days. The Bank of England was forced to intervene in the gilt market to prevent collateral calls on pension funds from triggering a systemic crisis.

Under modern debt dynamics, the ratio of debt to GDP evolves according to: d(Debt/GDP)/dt ≈ primary deficit + (r − g) × Debt/GDP, where r is the effective interest rate on debt and g is nominal GDP growth. For the UK in 2024, r ≈ 4% and g ≈ 4%, so (r − g) is close to zero – debt dynamics depend primarily on the primary balance. A 1pp rise in r with g unchanged adds roughly 1% of GDP to the debt trajectory each year through interest costs alone.

The IFS and OBR warn that the UK faces “limited fiscal headroom” – the ability to respond to future shocks without markets demanding a further risk premium. The Chancellor’s fiscal rules require debt/GDP to be falling in year five of the forecast window, constraining discretionary policy.

(Source: adapted from https://www.ifs.org.uk/publications/green-budget-2025)

Extract E
The Bank of England’s quantitative tightening

The Bank of England began Quantitative Tightening (QT) in 2022, selling gilts from the stock accumulated during QE (2009–2021). By mid-2025, the Bank’s balance sheet had reduced from a peak of £895 billion in gilts to around £580 billion. QT has imposed fiscal costs: the Treasury has indemnified the Bank for losses on gilt sales. In 2024, these indemnity payments reached £19 billion – a material drain on fiscal space.

Some economists argue QT worsens debt-interest costs unnecessarily. Others argue it is essential for restoring monetary-policy space and reducing long-run financial-stability risks from an oversized central-bank balance sheet. The Bank has indicated it intends to end active sales in 2026.

(Source: adapted from https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/news/2024/quantitative-tightening-review)

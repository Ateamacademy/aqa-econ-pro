Extract A
A tighter UK labour market

By mid-2022, the UK labour market had reached the tightest conditions on record: the vacancy rate exceeded 4% while unemployment fell below 3.8%. Employers across hospitality, logistics, agriculture and social care reported severe recruitment difficulties. Nominal wages grew at over 6% year-on-year, but real wages stagnated as CPI inflation reached double digits.

Commentators attributed the tightness to a combination of factors: a significant reduction in post-Brexit EU migration into lower-wage sectors, an unexpected rise in economic inactivity driven by long-term ill health among workers aged 50–64, and a faster-than-expected recovery in aggregate demand.

By 2025, some of these pressures had eased. EU labour-market participation partially recovered in sectors where visa rules had been relaxed, and the vacancy rate declined. However, the Bank of England continued to warn of second-round wage-price spiral risks, and the Monetary Policy Committee was slow to cut the Bank Rate despite falling headline inflation.

(Source: adapted from ONS and Bank of England MPC Minutes, 2025)

Extract B
Critical minerals, the energy transition and strategic concentration

Global demand for critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, nickel, rare earths and copper — has surged as the energy transition drives the scale-up of EV production, battery-storage installation and electricity-grid upgrades. The IEA estimates that demand for lithium could grow by 8–13 times by 2040 relative to a 2020 baseline.

Supply of several critical minerals is geographically concentrated. The Democratic Republic of the Congo produces approximately 70% of global cobalt; Chile and Australia together account for over 75% of global lithium extraction; Indonesia dominates nickel; China is responsible for the majority of downstream refining and processing of rare earths and lithium chemicals.

This concentration creates both opportunities and risks. For producing countries, sustained strong prices can generate substantial export revenues and foreign-exchange inflows. However, recent commodity-price history suggests that cycles are both pronounced and asymmetric: prices can triple within two years and then collapse by 60% over the following twelve months, as happened to lithium between 2022 and 2024.

Advanced economies have responded by launching 'friend-shoring' and supply-diversification initiatives, including the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (2024), which establishes strategic stockpiles and sets target benchmarks for domestic extraction, processing and recycling of critical minerals by 2030.

(Source: adapted from IEA Critical Minerals Outlook and World Bank commodity reviews, 2024)

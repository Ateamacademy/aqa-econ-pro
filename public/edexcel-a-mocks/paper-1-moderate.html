Extract A
Consolidation in the UK coffee-shop market

The UK high-street coffee-shop market has consolidated sharply over the past decade. Costa Coffee, which became the largest player after being acquired by Coca-Cola in 2019, now accounts for roughly a third of industry revenue. The market is dominated by three national chains, with local independents finding it increasingly difficult to compete with the scale, brand recognition and sophisticated loyalty apps of the big operators.

Prices across the main chains have risen by more than 25% since 2020, considerably faster than the rate of general consumer price inflation. Chains have cited higher wage bills, energy costs and the doubling of wholesale coffee-bean prices as the main drivers. Critics argue, however, that limited competition has allowed firms to pass costs through to consumers more fully than they might otherwise have done.

(Source: adapted from The Grocer, 2025)

Extract B
The rise of independent and local coffee shops

Alongside the big chains, the number of independent UK coffee shops has grown by around 15% since 2020. Many emphasise ethically sourced beans, non-standard menus and stronger local community ties. Independents often compete on quality rather than price, and tend to concentrate in larger cities, university towns and suburbs with high footfall.

Industry analysts suggest that consumer tastes are fragmenting. Younger customers in particular increasingly prefer speciality coffee, plant-based milks and locally owned outlets. Some commentators argue that this makes the high-street coffee market more competitive than market-share figures alone would suggest, because the big chains face pressure from fast-growing independents and at-home alternatives such as espresso pods.

(Source: adapted from industry trade publications, 2025)

Extract C
A proposed merger between Caffè Nero and Pret A Manger

In 2025 the owners of Caffè Nero and Pret A Manger announced exploratory talks on a possible merger. The combined business would overtake Starbucks to become the UK's second-largest chain by revenue, with around 21% market share. Supporters argue that the merger would deliver economies of scale in supply, marketing and staff training, and help the combined group compete more effectively with Costa Coffee.

Critics warn that the merger could reduce consumer choice in town centres where the two brands currently compete head-to-head, and that it may give the combined firm excessive bargaining power over dairy suppliers and coffee-bean farmers overseas. The Competition and Markets Authority has indicated it will scrutinise the proposal closely if it proceeds to a formal filing.

(Source: adapted from The Times business pages, 2025)

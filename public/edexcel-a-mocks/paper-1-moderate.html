Extract A
Discounters keep pressure on the Big Four

The UK grocery sector remains one of the most competitive retail markets in Europe. Aldi and Lidl together now hold over 18% of the grocery market, up from 12% in 2019. The discounters stock a narrower range of own-label products (around 1,800 SKUs compared with 35,000 at a typical Tesco superstore), enabling faster stock turnover and lower handling costs per unit.

Rising input costs have squeezed margins across the sector. Tesco's operating margin fell from 4.5% in 2022 to 3.9% in 2024. Sainsbury's responded by launching the “Aldi Price Match” scheme, which pledges to match the discounter's prices on over 750 essential items. Industry analysts question whether price-matching is profitable or whether it simply shifts the burden to suppliers.

Supermarkets also face rising staff costs. The UK National Living Wage rose by 9.8% in April 2024 and a further 6.7% in April 2025. Logistics costs have risen with fuel prices, and HGV driver shortages persist in parts of the country.

(Source: adapted from https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/business-supermarket-margins-2025)

Extract B
Online and convenience reshape the sector

The share of groceries bought online has stabilised at around 13% after the pandemic peak of 15.4%. Ocado Retail continues to grow its delivery-only model but has yet to reach operating profit. Meanwhile, convenience-store revenue (stores under 3,000 sq ft) has grown 14% since 2022 as consumers make smaller, more frequent shops.

Tesco Express, Sainsbury's Local and the Co-op dominate convenience, accounting for over 45% of the segment. Independent corner shops argue that large chains enjoy procurement and logistics advantages that make competitive pricing impossible for independents. A 2024 CMA market study found no evidence of anti-competitive behaviour in the convenience segment, but noted that local market concentration in some postcodes is very high.

(Source: adapted from https://www.gov.uk/cma-convenience-store-market-study-2024)

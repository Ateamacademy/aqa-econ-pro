Extract A
Consolidation in UK streaming

The UK streaming services market has matured rapidly since the early 2020s. Netflix remains the largest platform by subscribers, but growth has slowed dramatically: between 2023 and 2025 Netflix's UK subscriber base actually contracted by around 1.4 million despite ongoing price rises. Amazon, Disney and Apple continue to invest heavily in original content to retain subscribers.

In 2024 Paramount+ and Peacock merged their UK operations, reducing the number of major platforms from seven to six. Industry analysts argue the sector now resembles a tight oligopoly, with strong brand loyalty, high content-production costs acting as a barrier to entry, and coordinated pricing moves common across the larger players.

(Source: adapted from The Guardian, 2025)

Extract B
Price rises and subscriber fatigue

The average UK household now pays for 2.7 streaming subscriptions, down from 3.1 in 2022. Consumer research suggests that "subscription fatigue" has set in, with younger consumers in particular churning between services rather than holding multiple subscriptions simultaneously.

To combat this, several platforms have launched cheaper ad-supported tiers. Amazon introduced adverts as the default for Prime Video in 2024, with an ad-free option at an extra £2.99 per month. Netflix's ad-supported tier now accounts for around 35% of its UK sign-ups. Critics argue this is a form of second-degree price discrimination exploiting consumers with different willingness to pay.

(Source: adapted from The Times, 2025)

Extract C
A proposed acquisition of Channel 4 streaming by a US platform

In mid-2025 one of the major US streaming platforms announced exploratory talks to acquire the streaming operations of Channel 4, which currently operates the ad-supported All 4 / Channel 4 service in the UK. Supporters argue the deal would give Channel 4 the investment needed to compete in high-cost original drama production, securing British creative jobs.

Critics including the Voice of the Listener & Viewer and the House of Commons Culture Select Committee argue the merger would reduce the plurality of UK broadcasting, concentrate advertising revenue further, and weaken public-service content obligations. The Competition and Markets Authority has indicated it would examine the deal closely if it proceeds to a formal filing.

(Source: adapted from The Financial Times, 2025)

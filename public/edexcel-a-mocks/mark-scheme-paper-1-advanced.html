Question 4 — Tacit collusion / kinked demand

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
4(a)Kinked demand curve explanation:
  • Assumption: rivals match price cuts but not price rises.
  • Gives each firm a demand curve kinked at prevailing price: elastic above, inelastic below.
  • Discontinuity in MR at the kink — MC can fluctuate within the discontinuity without changing profit-max price.
  • Result: price rigidity even as costs change; firms avoid initiating price changes.
  • Incentive to compete on non-price dimensions (advertising, product differentiation, technology).

Question 5 — Platform data asymmetry & market failure

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
5(a)Information asymmetry → market failure:
  • Platform sees all sellers' prices, volumes, conversion rates, advertising spend; sellers see only own data.
  • Akerlof-style implications: platform can identify best-selling items and replicate them as own-brand.
  • Distorts competition: sellers face a competitor who knows their demand curve.
  • Reduces incentive for sellers to innovate (risk of copying) — allocative and dynamic inefficiency.
  • Market failure: asymmetric info prevents a level competitive playing field; mis-allocates productive resources; welfare loss.

Question 7 (EITHER) — Adequacy of microeconomic theory for platforms

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
7Evaluate:
  • Standard neoclassical models (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly) assume homogeneous products, one-sided markets, positive prices.
  • Digital platforms violate: multi-sided pricing, zero-price to one side, non-rivalrous data inputs, dynamic network effects.
  • Need for: two-sided market theory (Rochet-Tirole), data-as-input models, dynamic platform theory.
  • Behavioural economics adds crucial layer: default effects, attention economics, dark patterns.
  • Traditional welfare economics (consumer surplus) hard to measure for free services.
  • Counter: extended versions of existing theory (Cournot-with-network-effects; Stackelberg in platforms) can capture many effects.
  • Antitrust tools (HHI, merger analysis) partially inadequate but adaptable.
  • Innovation economics under-modelled: dynamic competition 'for the market' often more important than static competition within.
  • Judgement: existing theory provides necessary foundation but requires substantial extension; 'inadequate alone, insufficient alone' is the defensible position.

Question 8 (OR) — Behavioural economics and extensive intervention

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
8Evaluate:
  • Behavioural evidence: bounded rationality, loss aversion, hyperbolic discounting, choice overload well-documented.
  • Online exploitation: 'dark patterns' (Council report 2024), framing, default opt-ins, infinite-scroll design, personalised manipulation.
  • 'Systematically irrational' is too strong: deviations from full rationality are predictable but not universal or uniform.
  • Interventions: disclosure rules, cooling-off periods, prohibition of specific dark patterns (already in UK CPRs 2008; strengthened in 2024 Act).
  • Counter-argument: paternalism costs; limits consumer choice; slippery slope.
  • Evidence on intervention effectiveness mixed; nudges work for some domains (auto-enrolment) less for others.
  • Examples: buy-now-pay-later regulation; drip pricing ban; mandatory 'reject-all' cookie options.
  • Cost-benefit test: intervention warranted only where demonstrable harm and intervention proven effective.
  • Judgement: 'extensive' overstates; targeted, evidence-based intervention justified but 'extensive' blanket intervention is not.

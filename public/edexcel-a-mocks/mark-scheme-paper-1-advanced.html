Question 6(b) – Discuss the likely impact of rising content production costs on the profitability of UK streaming services (12 marks)

Diagram required: AC/MC curves shift upward due to content inflation; AR, MR unchanged; profit-maximising Q* falls, supernormal profit area shrinks.

KAA with diagram (out of 8)
  • AC/MC curves shift upward due to content inflation. With broadly downward-sloping AR (market power in differentiated oligopoly), profit-maximising output (MR=MC) falls and supernormal profit (P−AC)×Q shrinks.
  • Content costs have roughly doubled since 2019; Netflix alone spending \$17bn (Extract A).
  • Industry total \$140bn.
  • Smaller platforms (Paramount+, Apple TV+) face squeezed unit economics as fixed content costs amortised over fewer subscribers.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Largest players can spread costs over hundreds of millions of global subscribers (scale economies in content).
  • Password-sharing crackdowns and ad-tiers expand revenue to offset costs.
  • Industry bifurcates — top 3 viable, tail of 5–8 sub-scale.
  • Consolidation likely (Paramount/Skydance, Disney/Hulu).
  • Judgement: rising content costs compress margins across the sector, but the effect is highly uneven — leaders absorb the shock via scale and tier-pricing while sub-scale rivals face existential pressure and likely consolidation.

Question 6(c) – Examine two factors that may explain the rise in Netflix's UK ARPU (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Price rises: Netflix raised standard-tier prices multiple times between 2020 and 2024; relatively inelastic demand due to habit formation and content lock-in means revenue per user rises.
  • Tier mix shift: Netflix introduced an ad-supported tier (£4.99) and premium tiers (£17.99). Tier sorting captures consumer surplus via price discrimination.
  • Password-sharing crackdown: forcing separate subscriptions or £4.99 add-on fees converts previously free-riding viewers into paying customers → ARPU up.
  • Bundled add-ons: mobile game access, gaming expansion may justify higher-tier pricing.
  • Application: ARPU rose £9.89 → £12.04 = +21.7% over 4 years while core content library expanded.

Award 8-mark "Examine" band (KAA = 5, Eval = 3).

Question 6(d) – Assess whether Netflix is likely to be a profit maximiser (10 marks)

KAA (out of 6)

For profit maximisation:

  • Public company (NASDAQ: NFLX) with shareholder duty.
  • Price rises, password-sharing crackdown and ad-tier launch are all revenue- or profit-maximising moves.
  • MR = MC logic: tier pricing approximates third-degree price discrimination to maximise total profit.

Against / alternative objectives:

  • Content spend of \$17bn p.a. often outstrips operating cash flow — suggests sales or growth maximisation historically.
  • “Revenue maximisation” phase during subscriber-growth years; now shifting to profit maximisation as market matures.
  • Stakeholder theory: must balance talent/production partners vs. shareholders.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Firm behaviour changes over the business life-cycle — sales/growth in early stages, profit-max in maturity.
  • Public-company pressures make profit maximisation increasingly binding as analyst scrutiny rises.
  • “Winner-takes-most” phase (Extract A) increases urgency to extract profit before platform differentiation closes.
  • Judgement: Netflix's behaviour is consistent with long-run profit maximisation, with short-run phases of sales/growth maximisation to build market share. The 2023 crackdown and ad-tier launch signal transition to more conventional profit maximisation.

Question 6(e) – Discuss whether the UK streaming market operates closer to monopolistic competition or oligopoly (15 marks)

KAA (out of 9)

Features of MONOPOLISTIC COMPETITION:

  • Many differentiated products (genres, originals, libraries).
  • Low switching costs (cancel anytime); 62% of UK households subscribe to 2+.
  • Relatively low sunk costs for software platform (though content is enormous sunk cost).
  • Many consumers mix-and-match.

Features of OLIGOPOLY:

  • CR4 = 84% indicates high concentration.
  • Interdependence: competitive reaction to pricing (Netflix tier launch → Disney+ tier launch; Sky integration response).
  • High barriers to entry from content libraries (\$bn sunk costs).
  • Non-price competition: exclusive content, bundling, hardware integration (Sky Glass).
  • Ofcom concerned about algorithmic recommendation systems — structural feature of oligopoly.
Evaluation (out of 6)
  • Headline structure = concentrated oligopoly (CR4 = 84%).
  • Consumer experience has monopolistic-competition features (many branded services, differentiation, low switching costs).
  • Best described as a differentiated oligopoly — concentrated in supply but with meaningful product differentiation.
  • Pivot point: whether scale of content investment constitutes an insuperable entry barrier (supporting oligopoly classification) or whether low switching costs keep the market effectively competitive.
  • Judgement: the UK streaming market displays features of both structures but is closer to a differentiated oligopoly than monopolistic competition. Consumer-side features mask supply-side concentration that Ofcom's investigation is seeking to address.

Section C — Question 7 OR Question 8 (25 marks)

Question 7 – Evaluate whether natural monopolies should be publicly or privately owned (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Natural monopoly theory: LRAC falling over relevant output range, so single producer = efficient scale.
  • Private ownership: profit incentive → productive/dynamic efficiency; but may price above MC (allocative inefficiency), under-invest in less profitable routes.
  • Public ownership: can price at MC or average cost (allocative efficiency); strategic investment; but X-inefficiency, political capture, fiscal burden.
  • Regulation as hybrid: RPI−X price caps, ORR / Ofwat / Ofgem oversight.
  • Application (e.g. UK rail franchises, water, National Grid): specific evidence of fare rises > wages, leakage rates in water, Thames Water debt crisis.
  • Diagrams expected: natural monopoly AC/MC with AR/MR showing profit-max, socially optimal (P=MC) and average-cost pricing points.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Evidence base: privatised rail fares rose faster than wages, but investment also rose.
  • Water sector: mixed — Welsh Water (not-for-profit) compared to Thames Water (private) as natural experiment.
  • Depends on regulator strength and political commitment.
  • Short vs. long run: renationalisation has transition costs and potential value destruction.
  • Judgement: natural monopolies with strong network externalities and clear social obligations may be better under public ownership provided the government can credibly commit to investment and avoid short-termism. For others, well-regulated private ownership with transparent price caps can be superior. Regulatory capacity matters more than ownership form per se.

Question 8 – Evaluate the likely effectiveness of government intervention in addressing labour shortages (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Causes of shortages: demographic shifts, migration policy (post-Brexit), training pipeline lags, non-wage conditions.
  • Policy options: (i) raise wages in public sector; (ii) loosen migration visas; (iii) subsidise training; (iv) relax regulatory barriers; (v) improve working conditions.
  • Labour market theory: MRPL demand, supply elasticity, training lags, monopsony.
  • Application (e.g. NHS nurses, HGV drivers, construction trades): specific interventions — £5k bursaries, expanded visa routes, apprenticeship levy.
  • Diagram: labour supply/demand with shortage at existing wage; shift in Ls from training/migration.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Short-run ineffectiveness of training policies (lags of 2–5 years).
  • Migration policy faces political constraints.
  • Wage rises in the public sector have fiscal costs and can cause wage compression.
  • Unintended consequences: raising nurse pay may attract labour from the care sector (displacement).
  • Interaction with broader cost-of-living crisis.
  • Judgement: no single intervention resolves shortages; effective policy combines short-run migration/bursary mechanisms with long-run training and working-conditions reforms. Success depends on government's ability to commit across political cycles.

Question 1 — Generative AI productivity paradox

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
1(a)Productivity paradox + task-based model:
  • Productivity paradox: rapid technology adoption not reflected in measured aggregate productivity statistics — echo of 1987 Solow quip on computers.
  • Task-based labour model (Acemoglu & Autor / Acemoglu & Restrepo): jobs = bundles of tasks; technology automates tasks, not jobs.
  • Net employment effect = displacement (fewer tasks done by labour) minus productivity effect (task cheapening → output expansion → new tasks created).
  • Reinstatement effect: history shows new-technology eras have generated new labour-intensive tasks (cf. industrial revolution, computers).
  • AI-specific: generative AI automates cognitive tasks (writing, analysis, coding assistance) rather than routine manual — unusual historical reach.
  • Productivity paradox explanations: diffusion lag, intangible-capital investment period, mis-measurement, J-curve.

1(b)Prediction for graduate-occupation labour demand (0.8 task-automation share):

1(c)B — capital-labour substitution with strong task reinstatement. Historical pattern from prior GPT (general-purpose technology) waves — the technology automates existing tasks but also creates new ones, with polarising effects on wage distribution.5AO1/AO3
1(d)Why measured aggregate productivity has not risen despite rapid AI adoption:
  • Diffusion lag: new GPTs (general-purpose technologies) typically show 10–20 year lag between adoption and aggregate productivity gains (electricity, ICT).
  • Intangible-capital reallocation: firms spend heavily on complementary intangible investments (organisational change, skills, data) that are underweighted in TFP measurement — so we see costs now, productivity later.
  • J-curve effect: Brynjolfsson-Rock-Syverson — short-run productivity dips as firms reorganise, then rises sharply.
  • Measurement problems: free / near-free AI outputs not priced → not counted in GDP; quality improvements undercounted in deflators.
  • Misallocation: productivity gains concentrated in leading firms but lag behind in laggards; weighted average slow to move.
  • AI may genuinely be overhyped: real productivity gains may be narrower than enthusiast predictions (routine knowledge work, not production-function shift).
  • Evidence base: micro-level RCTs (customer support, coding) show 20–40% task-level gains; whether this aggregates to macro TFP remains an open question.
  • Policy response: patience required; premature conclusions of failure could drive bad policy (protectionism, over-regulation).
  • Counter-interpretation: if productivity does not rise, AI may be displacing labour without creating new productive capacity — redistribution without growth.

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
2Indicative content:
  • Growth / productivity: potential trend growth boost if AI realises 0.5–1.5pp TFP uplift (Goldman Sachs / McKinsey estimates).
  • Labour market: displacement in knowledge occupations; reinstatement uncertain; transition costs substantial.
  • Inequality: skill-biased technical change extended to cognitive workers → widened income dispersion.
  • Inflation: AI-driven productivity gains are disinflationary; but transition requires significant capital investment (short-run AD boost).
  • Monetary policy: raising r* if productivity trend rises; easier to avoid ZLB in future.
  • Fiscal: tax-base shifts (labour-income tax declines relative to capital income); corporate taxation and AI-compute-related taxes proposed.
  • Regional disparities: London / SE already highly exposed to AI opportunities; Northern / Welsh / NI employment structure more at-risk for routine cognitive displacement.
  • International competitiveness: UK tech stack (Oxford/Cambridge/DeepMind base) competitive but heavily dependent on US AI infrastructure.
  • Public services: NHS, education, civil service — large efficiency scope; transitions politically sensitive.
  • Risks: governance failures (misinformation, surveillance), macroeconomic concentration in few AI firms, data monopolies, AI safety tail risks.
  • Policy responses: active labour-market policies, skills investment, progressive tax-and-transfer reform, sovereign compute investment, pro-competition regulation.
  • Evaluation: direction of effects generally positive for aggregate productivity, but distribution sharply uneven; policy response determines whether net outcome positive for welfare. Question 3 — Mundell-Fleming trilemma, global financial cycle, capital-flow management

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
3(a)Mundell-Fleming 'impossible trinity':
  • Trilemma: only two of the following three can be achieved simultaneously — (1) fixed exchange rate, (2) free capital mobility, (3) independent monetary policy.
  • Small open EM with fixed peg + open capital account → must follow US monetary policy (loses (3)).
  • If tries to raise rates independently → capital inflows → FX intervention → money supply expands → defeats the tightening.
  • Alternative: floating exchange rate restores monetary independence (loses (1)).
  • Alternative: capital controls restore independence (loses (2)).
  • Case-study relevance: when US tightens, EM pegs come under pressure; emerging-market crises (1994 Mexico, 1997 Asia, 2013 taper tantrum) fit this pattern.

3(b)Rey's 'dilemma not trilemma' critique:
  • Rey (2013) argument: global financial cycle means capital flows are driven by US monetary policy regardless of receiving country's exchange-rate regime. Floating does NOT insulate.
  • Empirical evidence: asset prices, credit growth, leverage all co-move globally with US policy.
  • Trilemma becomes 'dilemma': either accept dollar-driven financial conditions or impose capital controls.
  • Implication: monetary independence under floating is illusory in practice.
  • Counter-evidence: some floating EMs (e.g., Brazil) do retain policy space; depends on credibility and FX depth.
  • Macroprudential policy as partial solution: counter-cyclical capital buffers, LTV limits, currency-mismatch regulation.
  • Capital-flow management (CFM): IMF 2012 revised view accepts CFM as legitimate tool under specific circumstances.
  • Trade-offs: CFM reduces allocative efficiency of capital; risks evasion; international coordination benefits.
  • Policy framework: integrate monetary, macroprudential, FX intervention, CFM (IMF Integrated Policy Framework).
  • EM-specific: dollar-denominated corporate debt exposes EM balance sheets to dollar cycle.

3(c)Evaluate: capital controls + capital-flow management as legitimate tools for EMs:
  • Case for CFM: global financial cycle makes floating insufficient; macroprudential alone doesn't always suffice; reduces sudden-stop risk; policy space for domestic stabilisation.
  • Theoretical basis: market failures in cross-border capital flows (contagion, externalities, pecuniary externalities from deleveraging).
  • Evidence on effectiveness: Chile's unremunerated reserve requirements (encaje) reduced inflow volumes but not shifts in composition; Brazilian IOF taxes during 2009-11 smoothed capital inflows; mixed evidence on Korea.
  • IMF evolution: from blanket opposition (1990s) to conditional acceptance (2012 Institutional View, 2022 IPF) that CFM is legitimate tool under specific conditions.
  • Against: efficiency costs (higher cost of capital); encourages evasion; reputational risks; undermines reform incentives in recipient countries; risk of misuse to prop up bad policies.
  • Historical concerns: CFM sometimes masks poor macro fundamentals rather than supplementing good ones.
  • Sequencing: CFM should be last resort; macroprudential first. Inflow CFMs less controversial than outflow CFMs.
  • International cooperation: source-country restrictions (limiting hot-money outflows from US/EU) economically more efficient but politically infeasible.
  • Target of policy: short-term speculative flows much easier to justify controlling than FDI.
  • BIS / IMF frameworks: currency-differentiated CFM, countercyclical application, targeted use.
  • Alternatives: deeper domestic financial development; local-currency bond markets; strong FX reserves; flexible exchange rates with FX intervention.
  • Sovereign-wealth buffers: Chile's copper-price stabilisation, Norway's Pension Fund — prudential alternatives to inflow controls.
  • Judgement: CFM / capital controls have a legitimate but circumscribed role in the EM toolkit — appropriate as temporary responses to surges and sudden stops, and as elements of a broader macroprudential framework, but not a substitute for sound fiscal / monetary / structural policy. Rey's dilemma points to need, but blanket embrace is unwarranted.

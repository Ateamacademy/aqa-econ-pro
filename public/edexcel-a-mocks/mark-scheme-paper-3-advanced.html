General Marking Guidance

All candidates must receive the same treatment. Examiners must mark the first candidate in exactly the same way as they mark the last. Mark schemes should be applied positively. Candidates must be rewarded for what they have shown they can do rather than penalised for omissions.

Crossed-out work should be marked unless the candidate has replaced it with an alternative response. All the marks on the mark scheme are designed to be awarded. Examiners should always award full marks if deserved, i.e. if the answer matches the mark scheme.

1(c) — 5 marks (MCQ)

Answer: C
New trade theory: trade between similar countries driven by scale economies + product variety; greatest gains when both can exploit scale and variety.

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Trade dimension: fragmentation reduces allocative efficiency; supply chains duplicated; higher costs passed to consumers; innovation gains from specialisation lost.
  • Gravity-model estimates: 1-2% global GDP loss from decoupling (IMF, McKinsey).
  • Finance dimension: bifurcated payments systems (CIPS, SWIFT); lower liquidity; higher transaction costs.
  • Reserve-asset fragmentation raises volatility and reduces insurance function of reserves.
  • CBDC proliferation: potentially disintermediating traditional banks; privacy concerns; settlement-speed gains.
  • Geostrategic implications: smaller countries forced to pick sides; lost benefits from multilateral integration.
  • Historical parallel: 1930s bloc formation (sterling, franc, dollar) preceded WW2.
  • Evaluation: complete decoupling unlikely; strategic decoupling in narrow sectors (chips, biotech) is more plausible and less costly than broad fragmentation.

Question 1(b) – Using a cost and revenue diagram, discuss the likely microeconomic effects on NVIDIA of entry by new GPU competitors (12 marks) MICRO ONLY

Diagram required: NVIDIA as dominant supplier with significant supernormal profit area; entry makes firm AR more elastic, MR shifts, profit-max Q changes, supernormal profit shrinks.

KAA (out of 8)
  • NVIDIA's 80% share and 60%+ gross margins consistent with dominant-firm pricing.
  • Barriers to entry: CUDA software moat (proprietary developer ecosystem); manufacturing partnerships (TSMC advanced nodes); network effects in developer tools.
  • New entry threats: AWS Trainium, Google TPU v5, AMD MI300X, Meta and Microsoft custom silicon.
  • Custom silicon from hyperscalers represents vertical integration reducing input dependence.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • CUDA ecosystem durable: switching costs for AI labs substantial.
  • Hyperscalers' custom silicon targets internal workloads; external market still NVIDIA-dominated.
  • But cloud providers represent 50%+ of NVIDIA revenue; their silicon shift is structurally damaging.
  • Price erosion probable in commodity segment; high-end AI GPUs retain pricing power.
  • Judgement: supernormal profit likely to compress over 3–5 years as hyperscalers internalise commoditised demand, but CUDA moat and tech lead keep margins elevated.

Question 1(c) – Examine two factors that may explain the “AI wage premium” for machine-learning engineers (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Inelastic labour supply: ML engineers require years of specialised training (PhDs, postgrads); supply near-vertical in short run.
  • High MRPL: each additional ML engineer can contribute disproportionately to model quality; winner-takes-most dynamics.
  • Winner-takes-most competition: hyperscalers and AI labs bid for the same small talent pool; bidding war pushes compensation above “market wage”.
  • Signing bonuses and equity: deferred compensation raises effective wage further.
  • Monopsony breakdown: employer pricing-power ineffective when well-funded firms bid for rare talent.
  • Application: UK Information and Communication wages +7.4% vs. 4.9% whole economy; US AI-adjacent roles +18%.

Question 1(d) – Evaluate the likely effects of the concentration of cloud infrastructure among three hyperscalers (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Oligopoly features: interdependence, non-price competition on performance, features, regional availability.
  • Barriers to entry: capital (>$1bn per data centre), software ecosystem, power/cooling expertise.
  • Economies of scale: natural-monopoly-like features in some sub-markets.
  • Allocative efficiency: P > MC, consumer surplus reduced by egress fees and lock-in structures.
  • Productive efficiency: hyperscalers achieve this through scale – but at cost of competition.
  • Dynamic efficiency: hyperscalers fund R&D ($100bn+ annual capex), drive frontier innovation.
KAA — MACRO
  • Investment spillovers: AI data-centre capex contributes 0.3–0.5pp to US GDP growth.
  • Productivity transmission: concentration of AI compute may slow diffusion of gains to wider economy.
  • Employment: direct jobs at data centres modest; indirect jobs via AI deployment.
  • Trade: cloud services as large export sector — US dominance captures global rents.
  • Financial-system risk: concentrated capex creates Dot-com-style correction risk if AI monetisation disappoints.

Evaluation:

  • Scale genuinely useful for performance — break-up may harm dynamic efficiency.
  • Concentration partly consequence of enormous R&D and capex; a cost of innovation.
  • BUT egress fees, tying, interoperability barriers look anticompetitive without efficiency justification.
  • Policy tools: price-cap egress fees; mandate interoperability; prohibit exclusive AI-model deals.
  • UK benefits from hyperscaler investment even if competition is weak — fiscal/political tension.
  • Judgement: hyperscaler concentration creates genuine consumer and competition harms warranting targeted regulatory intervention (egress fees, interoperability), but wholesale structural remedies would reduce dynamic efficiency and cause short-term macro damage via reduced capex. “Conduct” rather than “structure” remedies preferred.

Question 1(e) – Evaluate the likely consequences of rapid AI-driven automation for labour markets and income distribution (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Labour market: rapid fall in demand for routine-cognitive labour; rising demand for AI-adjacent skills.
  • Real wages: bifurcation — premium for AI-skilled (7.4–18% gains), stagnation for displaced.
  • Income distribution: capital share rises; labour share may fall further.
  • Substitutability vs. complementarity: AI as substitute (displaces) vs. complement (augments); task-level analysis (Acemoglu-Restrepo).
  • Firm-level: 20–40% task-level gains for early adopters.
KAA — MACRO
  • Productivity transmission: aggregate gains lag micro gains (Solow paradox recurrence).
  • AD composition: rising investment share, declining labour share → lower MPC, weaker multiplier.
  • Inflation: productivity gains disinflationary if passed through; concentrated rents may keep prices high.
  • Fiscal: displaced workers need retraining; tax base challenges.
  • Growth: potential step-change if transmission occurs; alternatively dot-com disappointment.

Evaluation:

  • IMF's 40% exposed and 60% of exposed at substitution risk in developed economies is dramatic — but “exposed” ≠ “displaced”.
  • Historical analogy: previous automation waves (ATMs, computers) affected occupations but employment recovered via new task creation.
  • But AI reaches cognitive tasks previously insulated — unclear what “new task creation” looks like.
  • Short-run: concentrated gains among high-skilled and capital-owning; broad disruption in routine-cognitive occupations.
  • Policy needed: reskilling, active labour-market policy, taxation reform.
  • Judgement: AI automation presents genuine risks of faster and broader disruption than previous waves; distribution of gains is the critical variable. Policy priority is managing transition via investment in retraining, not resisting the technology.

Question 2(b) – Examine two factors that may explain the UK's persistently weak productivity growth since 2008 (8 marks)

  • Weak capital deepening: UK business investment consistently below G7 peers as share of GDP; insufficient investment per worker slows productivity growth.
  • Poor technology diffusion: productivity gap between “frontier” firms and long tail of UK firms has widened since 2008.
  • Management quality: international comparisons find UK firms lag US, Germany, Japan; UK “tail” particularly weak.
  • Brexit trade frictions: goods exporters face new non-tariff barriers; affected firms invest less.
  • Planning-system constraints: infrastructure delays — energy grid, transport, housing — reduce agglomeration benefits.
  • Skills: digital skills gap; apprenticeship levy funds often go to management qualifications rather than technical skills.
  • Regional imbalance: productivity gap between London/SE and the rest widened.

Question 2(c) – Discuss the likely microeconomic and macroeconomic effects on UK firms and the UK economy of a large rise in business investment driven by “full expensing” (12 marks) MICRO + MACRO

Microeconomic
  • Firm-level cost of capital: full expensing reduces effective tax-adjusted cost of a capital asset by up to 25%.
  • Firm-level NPV hurdle: more projects become positive-NPV.
  • Sector heterogeneity: capital-intensive sectors (manufacturing, infrastructure) benefit more than labour-intensive services.
  • Firm-size heterogeneity: larger firms with tax liabilities benefit; loss-making start-ups gain less.
  • Equipment suppliers benefit as secondary beneficiaries.
Macroeconomic (AD/AS)
  • AD shifts right: I is component of AD; multiplier through supplier incomes and consumption.
  • LRAS rightward in long run: higher capital stock → higher labour productivity → higher potential output.
  • OBR estimate: 3% steady-state lift to business investment.
  • Two-stage outcome: short run (↑Y, ↑P) via AD; long run (↑Y without P pressure) via LRAS.
  • Fiscal cost: ~£10bn forgone CT revenue; crowding-out possible.

Evaluation:

  • OBR 3% is modest – from ~£203bn to ~£209bn.
  • Micro deadweight: firms that would have invested anyway receive windfall.
  • Binding UK constraints (planning, demand, management) largely unaddressed.
  • Policy-horizon: 5–10 year capex requires durability, which is uncertain.
  • Judgement: micro positive for capital-intensive firms; macro modest short-run and small long-run uplift. Policy works at the margin, not transformatively.

Candidates must cover both micro AND macro for L3.

Question 2(d) – Evaluate the likely effectiveness of the UK government's supply-side package in raising long-run trend growth (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Full expensing: reduces cost of capital → higher firm-level investment.
  • Apprenticeship levy reform: addresses labour-market friction.
  • Planning reform: reduces transaction costs in infrastructure, housing, energy.
  • Skills bootcamps: targeted human capital investment in digital/AI.
  • Diagrams: supply-side shifts in specific markets; labour market.
KAA — MACRO
  • LRAS shifts right → higher trend growth, lower equilibrium inflation.
  • Productivity channel: capital deepening + skills + infrastructure.
  • Trend target 1.7% (from 1.3%) – 0.4pp uplift over a decade.
  • Fiscal feedback: higher growth → higher tax revenue → improves debt dynamics.
  • Inflation: supply-side expansion is disinflationary.

Evaluation:

  • IFS/OBR “plausible but ambitious” — consistent with modest (0.1–0.3pp) rather than transformative effects.
  • Binding UK constraints (R&D intensity, regional imbalance, management quality) beyond scope of package.
  • Execution risk: apprenticeship levy underperformed in past; planning reform faces political resistance.
  • International comparison: US IRA and European equivalents are larger and more targeted.
  • Long lead time (10+ years) means risk of reversal.
  • Judgement: package directionally correct; planning reform most significant element. Could close ~half the gap between current and pre-2008 trend growth if sustained. Deeper structural issues remain.

Question 2(e) – Evaluate the case for substantial further cuts in the BoE's Bank Rate to support UK economic growth (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Consumer: lower borrowing costs → higher consumption (durables, housing).
  • Firms: lower cost of capital → higher investment.
  • Labour market: lower rates may sustain employment but also keep market tight.
  • Housing market: rate cuts relax mortgage affordability constraint.
KAA — MACRO
  • AD/AS: cuts shift AD right; in tight labour market with sticky services inflation, risks domestic price pressure.
  • Exchange rate: cuts depreciate sterling → higher import prices.
  • Inflation expectations: cuts risk un-anchoring if wage-price dynamics embedded.
  • Growth-inflation trade-off: cuts support weak growth at cost of slower inflation return.
  • Monetary-fiscal: with debt 99%+ of GDP, higher rates costly; cuts support fiscal.

Evaluation:

  • Services inflation 4.5% is serious constraint; labour market tight (4.5% unemployment, elevated vacancies).
  • Counter: transmission slow (fixed-rate mortgages), so effective tightening smaller than nominal suggests.
  • Supply-side package is appropriate growth lever; monetary policy should focus on inflation mandate.
  • International comparison: ECB and Fed cutting; BoE pressure to follow avoids unwanted sterling appreciation.
  • Distribution: cuts benefit borrowers over savers.
  • Judgement: substantial cuts not clearly warranted. A gradual, cautious cutting cycle is preferable. Growth support should come from supply-side and fiscal policy, not aggressive monetary easing.

1(b)Competitive labour market: minimum wage above equilibrium:
  • Diagram: DL (downward-sloping), SL (upward-sloping), equilibrium W*, Q*.
  • Minimum wage Wmin > W* → employment (QD) falls below Q*.
  • Labour supply at Wmin exceeds labour demand → excess supply / classical unemployment.
  • Area of unemployment = QS(Wmin) − QD(Wmin).
  • Size of employment loss depends on (|PEDL|, |PESL|) and extent to which Wmin exceeds W*.

1(d)Why NLW impact on total employment has been smaller than early critics feared:
  • Monopsonistic elements in many UK low-wage labour markets (care, retail, hospitality).
  • Firms have multiple margins of adjustment: reduce hours, raise prices, reduce non-wage perks, increase productivity demand, reduce vacancies filled.
  • Demand elasticity for labour in affected sectors often low: close substitution with capital slow.
  • Efficiency-wage effects: higher wages reduce turnover, raise productivity.
  • Aggregate demand effect: NLW workers have high MPC → wage rises raise C → demand for goods, offsetting employment cost.
  • Prices passed through to consumers (not unemployment) in many sectors.
  • Evidence: Card-Krueger research and UK Low Pay Commission reviews.

SECTION B — 25 marks

Question 2 — 20% further rise in NLW by 2030 — evaluate

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
2Indicative content — micro and macro effects:
  • Micro: compressed wage distribution; reduced wage premiums for skilled workers; incentive to substitute capital for labour in capital-deep-intensive sectors.
  • Affected sectors: hospitality, retail, social care; regional disparities — bigger impact in lower-wage regions (NE, Wales, NI).
  • Monopsony analysis: low end of labour market often monopsonistic; minimum wage can raise W and L.
  • Macro: raises AD via C (NLW workers have MPC ≈ 0.7–0.8).
  • Cost-push pressure: raises SRAS costs → higher CPI; may require tighter monetary response.
  • Productivity: efficiency-wage channel; incentive to mechanise low-paid tasks.
  • Public finances: higher tax / NI receipts; lower in-work benefit payments.
  • Distributional: reduces in-work poverty; progressive income effect.
  • Competitiveness: raises unit labour costs; international trade impact (mostly non-tradable sectors unaffected).
  • Informal economy: risk of informalisation in very low-margin sectors.
  • Evaluation: size of employment-loss depends on monopsony power vs competitive-market prevalence; net effect positive in sectors with monopsony, less clear in tradeable sectors; much depends on pace and how it interacts with productivity.

Question 3 — Eurozone inflation and ECB monetary policy

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
3(a)Cause of sharp rise in eurozone inflation 2022:
  • External supply shock: Russia's invasion of Ukraine disrupted natural-gas supply; wholesale gas prices rose 400%+.
  • AD/AS diagram: SRAS shifts left; energy-intensive goods and services prices rise; aggregate CPI lifted.
  • Imported-inflation channel: eurozone heavily dependent on imported energy.
  • Secondary effects: wage-price pass-through via collective bargaining; services inflation followed.
  • Global supply-chain disruption post-pandemic compounded the shock.
  • Some demand-pull element from pandemic-savings unwinding but supply-side dominant.

3(b)ECB rate rise −0.5% → 4% micro and macro effects:
  • Macro: AD compression via consumption (higher mortgage rates in variable-rate countries like Spain), investment, exchange-rate (euro strengthened).
  • Inflation effect: CPI fell from 10.6% peak back towards 2% target by 2024 — relatively successful disinflation.
  • Cost: growth slowed materially; Germany entered technical recession 2023.
  • Micro: distributional impact varies across member states — Italy/Spain with high mortgage-rate sensitivity vs Germany's mostly-fixed mortgages.
  • Small firms more credit-constrained than large; heterogeneous effects on investment.
  • Fiscal implications: higher sovereign yields widened spreads (Italy BTP-Bund spread); debt-sustainability concerns for periphery.
  • Banking sector: NIM benefit but credit-quality deterioration risk.
  • Effectiveness & evaluation: arguably late to tighten (2022 start), then aggressive; communication on 'data-dependence' generally credible; inflation differentials persist.

3(c)Evaluate: can single monetary policy effectively control inflation across all eurozone members?
  • Central argument: OCA theory suggests single MP only optimal if shocks symmetric, mobility high, or fiscal transfers large. Eurozone fails on multiple criteria.
  • Evidence of divergence: Slovak/Baltic inflation stickier than French/Italian (Extract B); dispersion of 4%+ at peak.
  • Asymmetric shocks common: energy-dependency varies; fiscal space varies; economic structure differs (manufacturing vs services).
  • One-size-fits-all rate: tight for weaker members, loose for booming members.
  • Channels differ: high variable-rate mortgage share in Spain vs fixed-rate in Germany → very different transmission.
  • Pre-EMU alternative: independent monetary policy adjusted to each nation's cycle — but at cost of exchange-rate volatility and reduced trade integration.
  • National-level tools available: fiscal policy (within SGP limits), macroprudential policy, wage-bargaining reforms.
  • Historical record: ECB has maintained inflation target on average; dispersion has not destabilised union.
  • Structural solutions: banking union, fiscal capacity (NGEU), labour-market reform — all progress, none complete.
  • Counter-view: currency union benefits (trade integration, price transparency, reduced xrate volatility) may outweigh monetary-policy costs.
  • Judgement: single MP cannot perfectly control inflation across all members; however, improvements to transmission and complementary policies have made it workable. Not optimal but not destabilising.

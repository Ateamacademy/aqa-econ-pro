Question 1(b) – Using a cost and revenue diagram, discuss the likely microeconomic effects on Octopus Energy's growth in market share since 2016 (12 marks) MICRO ONLY

Indicative content (a diagram is required; candidates may choose a cost/revenue, market-share, or game-theory representation):

KAA (out of 8)
  • Cost/revenue diagram: Octopus with lower AC curve than Big Six (reflecting technology-driven cost advantage); lower price consistent with supernormal profit.
  • Technology platform (“Kraken”) reduces servicing costs per customer; scale economies in software.
  • Brand trust built during 2021–22 crisis while 29 suppliers went bust.
  • Acquisition of failed Bulb (2022) added ~1.5m customers at low cost.
  • Renewable-only positioning differentiates the product (monopolistic-competition element).
  • Price cap limits incumbents' pricing power – entrants compete on service/brand.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Some growth came via acquisition, not pure organic growth.
  • Fixed-price tariffs carry hedging risk – several challengers failed doing the same.
  • Winner's curse if sector margins remain capped by Ofgem.
  • Long-run sustainability depends on whether the tech advantage is durable or copied.
  • Judgement: gains reflect a genuine productivity edge plus acquisition opportunism; scale advantages may cap further growth unless adjacent markets (EVs, smart grids, home services) succeed.

Question 1(c) – Examine two reasons why coal has almost disappeared from UK electricity generation (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Carbon pricing: UK carbon price (UK ETS + Carbon Price Support) at ~£42/t in 2024, peaked at £98/t in 2022. Raises coal's marginal cost above gas's (gas emits ~40% less CO₂/MWh). Shifts merit order – coal pushed off grid.
  • Relative fuel cost shift: US shale-gas export growth from 2010s reduced global gas prices; for most of 2015–2021 gas was structurally cheaper per MWh than coal after carbon price.
  • Regulatory commitment: UK announced closure of all coal plants by 2024 (Drax last unit closed Sep 2024); long-term certainty meant no further investment in coal maintenance.
  • Renewables displacement: wind+solar share rose from ~15% (2015) to ~35% (2024). Near-zero marginal cost of renewables pushes coal off grid first during high renewables output.
  • Air-quality regulation: EU Industrial Emissions Directive required costly SO₂/NOₓ abatement, making old coal plants uneconomic to retrofit.

Award 8-mark “Examine” band (KAA=5, Eval=3).

Question 1(d) – Evaluate the likely impact of the Ofgem price cap on consumer welfare and supplier behaviour (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA (out of 15) — MICRO
  • Price cap as maximum-price regulation: limits SVT price to regulator-set level.
  • Effect on consumer surplus: protects against monopolistic pricing; but limits cheap-deals segment that existed pre-cap.
  • Supplier exit: 29 suppliers failed 2021–22; implicit cross-subsidy via the “failed supplier” pot.
  • Competition: reduces incentive to offer tariffs much below cap; fewer cheap fixed tariffs.
  • Diagram: price ceiling showing shortage if binding below equilibrium; or cost/revenue showing constrained supernormal profit.
KAA (out of 15) — MACRO
  • Effect on CPI: cap smooths energy CPI by delaying pass-through (both up and down).
  • Aggregate consumption: cap shield during 2022 limited real-income destruction vs. unregulated scenario.
  • Monetary policy: smoothed energy inflation affected the MPC's response pace.
  • Fiscal: government Energy Price Guarantee cost ~£30bn alongside cap.
  • Inflation expectations: cap may have helped keep expectations anchored.

Evaluation (out of 10):

  • Cap clearly benefited consumers during the 2022 crisis – hundreds of pounds per household avoided.
  • But cap contributed to supplier failures by not allowing fast cost pass-through.
  • Long run: reduces competitive dynamism; suppliers bunch at the cap.
  • Distributional: cap helps all consumers (not targeted); non-bill-paying vulnerable groups benefit less in relative terms.
  • Alternative tools: direct income support (more targeted), social tariff, carbon dividend.
  • Judgement: price cap is a blunt instrument appropriate for crisis periods but corrosive of competition in normal times; best reformed into a “backstop only” cap with active competition policy restored.

Question 1(e) – Evaluate the case for using a carbon price (rather than regulation) as the primary tool for reducing CO2 emissions (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA (out of 15) — MICRO
  • Carbon price internalises externalities (Pigouvian tax logic).
  • Equates marginal abatement cost across sectors → productive efficiency in emissions cuts.
  • Diagram: externality diagram showing MPC → MPC+tax toward socially optimal Q*.
  • Regulation forces specific abatement regardless of cost.
  • Price discovery: market participants reveal true abatement costs.
KAA (out of 15) — MACRO
  • Carbon revenue: UK raised ~£6bn/year from UK ETS + CPS in 2024.
  • Inflation: carbon price raises energy prices — contributes to CPI.
  • Competitiveness: UK unilateral carbon price may disadvantage tradable sectors; CBAM partly addresses.
  • Green investment: carbon price signals renewables, storage, hydrogen investment.
  • Distribution: poorer households spend higher share of income on energy → regressive absent rebates.

Evaluation (out of 10):

  • Coal elimination (22.5% → 0.4%) is clear supporting evidence that carbon price works in electricity.
  • But price alone insufficient for deeper decarbonisation: grid, planning, storage all need regulation/subsidy.
  • Regulation provides certainty that price signals cannot (investors need 20+ year horizons).
  • Mixed approach (price + regulation + subsidy) more effective than either alone.
  • Price volatility (£42 → £98 → £42 per tonne) undermines investment planning.
  • Judgement: carbon price is highly effective as a least-cost abatement instrument in competitive markets with many substitutes (as in UK electricity); less suitable where investment lumpiness and capital vintages matter. “Essential tool among several” is the defensible position.

Question 2(b) – Examine two factors that may have slowed the transmission of the BoE's monetary tightening to the UK economy (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Fixed-rate mortgages: 85%+ of UK mortgages fixed 2–5 years; only ~1.5m re-fixed in 2024. Household cash-flow effect drawn out over several years.
  • Corporate debt structures: larger firms largely fixed-rate or bond-financed; small firms on floating rates feel impact faster.
  • Wealth effects weaker: fewer housing transactions due to higher rates → less mortgage equity withdrawal.
  • Expectations channel: when inflation expectations un-anchor, the required central bank action is larger for a given disinflation.
  • Savings/deposit rates: slow pass-through to savers – banks retained spread.
  • Fiscal offsetting: 2022 Energy Price Guarantee and tax cuts offset part of the tightening.

Award 8-mark “Examine” band (KAA=5, Eval=3).

Question 2(c) – Discuss the likely microeconomic and macroeconomic effects on UK households and firms of the BoE's rate cuts from 5.25% to 4.50% (12 marks) MICRO + MACRO

Indicative content (a diagram is required; AD/AS, loanable funds, or labour/firm-level diagrams are all acceptable):

Microeconomic
  • Household cost of borrowing: mortgage re-fixers gain; £240/month payments avoided (Extract D reference).
  • Savers' deposit rates: deposit-holder income reduced; distributional effect.
  • Firm investment: lower cost of capital → more positive-NPV projects.
  • Sector heterogeneity: capital-intensive sectors (housing, construction) gain more than services.
  • Housing market: mortgage affordability improves → transactions rise → estate agents, removals firms benefit.
Macroeconomic (AD/AS)
  • AD shifts right: lower rates → ↑C + ↑I + ↑X (sterling depreciation); new equilibrium at higher Y, higher P.
  • Multiplier effect: extra spending generates additional income through rounds.
  • Sterling effect: lower rates → capital outflow → depreciation → imported inflation + exporter boost.
  • Labour market: higher AD → reduced unemployment.
  • Transmission lag: fixed-rate mortgages delay effect; ~1.5m re-fix per year.

Evaluation:

  • Magnitude: 75bp cut from 5.25% to 4.50% is modest; stance still restrictive.
  • Services inflation at 4–5% risks being reignited.
  • Business investment constrained by planning/Brexit more than by rates.
  • Micro distributional unevenness: savers lose, borrowers win.
  • Judgement: micro gains concentrated among households with variable/re-fixing mortgages and capital-intensive firms; macro support for AD is modest and insufficient alone to restore trend growth without complementary supply-side action.

Candidates must cover both micro AND macro to access L3 on KAA.

Question 2(d) – Evaluate the likely effectiveness of the supply-side reforms described in Extract E in raising UK long-run growth (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA (out of 15) — MICRO
  • Full expensing: reduces effective cost of capital → higher firm-level investment, especially capital-intensive firms.
  • Apprenticeship levy reform: addresses labour-market friction; aligns skills supply to employer demand.
  • Planning reform: reduces transaction costs for infrastructure, housing, energy; lowers sunk costs for large projects.
  • Investment zones: generate agglomeration economies locally.
  • Diagrams: supply-side shifts in specific markets; labour-market diagrams showing Ls shift.
KAA (out of 15) — MACRO
  • LRAS shifts right → higher trend growth, lower equilibrium inflation for given AD.
  • Productivity channel: capital deepening + skills + infrastructure.
  • Target of 1.7% trend (from 1.3%) — 0.4pp uplift over a decade.
  • Fiscal feedback: higher growth → higher tax revenue → improved debt dynamics.
  • Supply-side expansion is disinflationary; compatible with more dovish monetary stance.

Evaluation (out of 10):

  • IFS/OBR: “plausible but ambitious” — consistent with modest (0.1–0.3pp) rather than transformative effects.
  • Binding UK constraints (low R&D, weak management, regional imbalance) not fully addressed.
  • Execution risk: apprenticeship levy has underperformed past reforms; planning reform faces political resistance.
  • Interaction with macro conditions: supply-side effective only if demand is adequate.
  • Long time horizon (10+ years) means risk of reversal by future governments.
  • Judgement: package is directionally correct and, if fully implemented, could close roughly half the gap between current and pre-2008 trend growth. Planning reform is the single most significant measure. But deeper structural issues remain; package is a down-payment on trend-growth recovery, not a complete solution.

Question 2(e) – Evaluate whether the BoE's primary mandate should remain the 2% CPI inflation target given the UK's weak growth performance since 2010 (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA (out of 15) — MICRO
  • Inflation distortion: menu costs, tax-bracket drift, wealth transfers between creditors/debtors.
  • Inflation expectations: anchoring role of a credible target.
  • Misallocation: high/volatile inflation distorts relative prices and long-term contracting.
  • Tinbergen rule: one instrument cannot credibly deliver both low inflation AND high employment.
KAA (out of 15) — MACRO
  • Mandate options: (i) inflation target only (current UK); (ii) dual mandate (Fed); (iii) NGDP targeting; (iv) higher (e.g. 3%) inflation target.
  • Arguments for change: weak UK growth partly reflects over-tight policy; fixed target creates ZLB risk; services inflation has different dynamics from goods inflation.
  • Arguments against change: credibility built over 30 years; changing target signals institutional weakness; growth problem is supply-side not monetary.

Evaluation (out of 10):

  • Mandate credibility critical – changing it during inflation return to target would be poor timing.
  • UK's weak growth is structural (productivity, investment) not cyclical → monetary policy can't fix it.
  • Counter: ZLB post-2009 meant monetary policy couldn't support recovery adequately; higher target gives more room.
  • Dual mandate risk: politicisation of central bank if employment target added.
  • NGDP targeting: theoretically attractive but measurement lags undermine real-time.
  • Judgement: on balance, the 2% CPI target should be retained – evidence that weak UK growth reflects supply-side rather than demand-side problems means changing the monetary mandate is unlikely to address the problem and risks credibility. Reform of the mandate is a lower-priority policy lever than planning, productivity and investment reform.

Question 7 (EITHER) — £5 per pack sin-tax on disposable vapes

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
7Evaluate microeconomic effects:
  • Negative-externality framework: information failure and external costs (health, littering); tax aims to internalise MSC.
  • Diagram: SMC above SMB → socially optimal output Q* < free-market Qm; tax raises supply curve (S + tax).
  • Demand-side: price elasticity of demand for disposables, especially for young (addictive element; lower incomes → may be more elastic).
  • Incidence: how much of the £5 passes through to consumers vs producers depends on relative elasticities.
  • Substitution: towards reusable vapes, smuggled products, informal market, or tobacco (perverse outcome).
  • Equity: regressive impact on low-income smokers/vapers; but health gains may be progressive.
  • Administrative feasibility; enforcement costs; possible hypothecation of revenue for cessation services.
  • Evaluation: effectiveness at reducing youth uptake vs revenue vs black-market creation; compared with alternatives (flavour ban, display restrictions).

Question 8 (OR) — "Small firms can never compete effectively against large established brands"

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
8Evaluate the claim:
  • Arguments FOR: large-firm advantages — EoS (technical, marketing, purchasing), brand loyalty, distribution networks, access to capital.
  • Natural barriers: MES, sunk costs; strategic barriers: advertising, patents, predatory pricing.
  • Arguments AGAINST: differentiation, agility, niche markets, local presence, craft/ethical branding.
  • Internet / D2C / social media has lowered marketing and distribution costs for small firms.
  • Examples (candidates should choose one industry): craft beer vs major brewers; independent coffee shops vs chains; D2C fashion brands vs established retailers; fintech challengers vs major banks.
  • Market structure matters: contestable markets; role of competition policy (CMA).
  • Behavioural factors: 'small is beautiful' preference; authenticity premium.
  • Evaluation: 'never' is too strong; depends on industry characteristics, MES relative to market size, degree of product differentiation, contestability.

Question 6(b) – Discuss the likely impact of rising input and staff costs on the profitability of UK supermarkets (12 marks)

Diagram required: cost and revenue diagram showing AC1, MC1 shifting upwards to AC2, MC2.

KAA with diagram (out of 8)
  • Cost/revenue diagram: AC and MC shift up due to rising input prices and wages; with AR/MR unchanged, profit-maximising output Q* falls and supernormal profit area (P−AC)×Q shrinks.
  • Tesco margin fell 60bps (4.5% → 3.9%).
  • National Living Wage up 9.8% then 6.7% — labour is a major variable cost (typically 10–12% of revenue).
  • Wheat price up ~34% from April 2024 trough.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Supermarkets have some pricing power to pass costs on, especially on non-competitive SKUs.
  • Intense competition from discounters and price-matching schemes limits pass-through.
  • Large chains may respond via automation (self-checkout, robotics) or by squeezing suppliers.
  • Judgement: rising costs are clearly margin-compressing but firms with scale and loyalty advantages (Tesco) can absorb more than weaker competitors. Mid-tier players (Morrisons, Asda) most at risk of structural damage.

Question 6(c) – Examine two factors that may cause significant changes in the global price of wheat (8 marks)

Indicative content (at least two distinct factors developed):

  • Supply-side shocks: adverse weather, geopolitical conflict disrupting Black Sea exports, fertiliser price rises. Wheat supply is price-inelastic in the short run (planting-to-harvest lag), so supply shifts produce large price changes.
  • Demand-side factors: rising global population, biofuel demand, speculative futures trading, income growth in emerging markets raising demand for meat (and hence feed wheat). Demand is income-inelastic in developed markets, more elastic in developing ones.
  • Speculation and storage: wheat inventories held by traders; expectations of future shortages amplify price swings.
  • Application to Figure 2: prices rose from US\$218 (Apr 2024) to US\$292 (Jan 2025), a ~34% rise — consistent with a supply-side shock hitting an inelastic market.

Award 8-mark "Examine" band (KAA = 5, Eval = 3).

Question 6(d) – Assess the likely impact on Tesco of Aldi and Lidl's rising market share (10 marks)

KAA (out of 6)
  • Tesco's operating margin falling from 4.5% to 3.9% shows clear margin compression.
  • Price-matching schemes (Aldi Price Match) reduce revenue per unit.
  • Narrow Aldi/Lidl range (1,800 SKUs) gives them lower unit costs Tesco can't match at scale.
  • Tesco's non-price differentiation (Clubcard, range, online) remains a defensive moat.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Short-run margin damage is real but long-run Tesco still leads market at 27.9%.
  • Loyalty-scheme data (Clubcard) is a competitive advantage discounters cannot easily replicate.
  • Sustained discounter growth (12% → 18% since 2019) points to structural shift in consumer preferences post-cost-of-living crisis.
  • Judgement: impact is margin-compressing rather than share-destroying for Tesco; the firms most at risk from discounter expansion are mid-tier players, not the market leader.

Question 6(e) – Discuss whether the UK grocery market is contestable (15 marks)

KAA (out of 9)

Contestability framework: low entry barriers, hit-and-run entry possible, low sunk costs, pool of potential entrants, no strategic barriers.

Evidence FOR contestability:

  • Aldi and Lidl's successful entry and growth from <6% combined (2010) to >18% (2025).
  • Ocado Retail shows online-only model is viable.
  • Convenience segment sees entry by symbol groups (Nisa, Premier).

Evidence AGAINST contestability:

  • Top 4 control 64.3% of market.
  • Huge sunk costs (distribution centres, store freeholds) — not “hit-and-run” markets.
  • Brand loyalty (Clubcard data).
  • Land-banking and restrictive covenants on retail sites act as strategic entry barriers.
  • Procurement scale advantages make new entrants vulnerable.
Evaluation (out of 6)
  • Market shows SOME features of contestability at segment level (discounters at low-price end; Ocado in premium online) but the headline national market remains a tight oligopoly.
  • Contestability is partial and uneven — strong in some segments, weak in others.
  • Convenience and online are more contestable than big-box grocery.
  • Discounter success may overstate contestability — Aldi and Lidl entered over decades, not years.
  • Judgement: the UK grocery market is imperfectly contestable — not a textbook contestable market, but sufficiently open at segment level that the dominant firms face meaningful competitive pressure. Regulatory vigilance (CMA) matters to preserve what contestability exists.

Question 7 – Evaluate whether a fall in the number of firms in an oligopolistic market is likely to harm consumers (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Oligopoly theory: kinked demand curve, tacit and overt collusion, game theory.
  • Concentration ratio, barriers to entry, non-price competition.
  • Consumer surplus, allocative/productive efficiency, dynamic efficiency.
  • Regulatory framework (CMA, Ofcom).
  • Application to named industry (e.g. UK mobile networks post-Vodafone/Three merger): concentration rises from CR4 = 100% to CR3 = 100% but with fewer rivals; potential impact on prices, investment in 5G, innovation.
  • Diagram expected: oligopoly equilibrium vs. more-competitive counterfactual, showing P > MC gap widening.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • Fewer firms ≠ worse outcomes if dynamic efficiency gains (economies of scale in 5G rollout funded by stronger balance sheets) outweigh static losses.
  • Merger remedies (spectrum divestment) can mitigate harm.
  • Counter: collusion easier with fewer players; non-price competition may substitute.
  • Depends on market structure — if still contestable, harm limited.
  • Depends on regulatory strength.
  • Judgement: on balance, in heavily regulated markets with effective competition authorities, harm is limited; in weakly regulated markets, real consumer detriment likely. Context-specific rather than universal.

Question 8 – Evaluate the factors that determine wage rates in an occupation of your choice (25 marks)

KAA (out of 15)
  • Marginal revenue product of labour (MRPL) theory: wage = MRPL in competitive labour market.
  • Demand for labour: derived demand, MRPL = MPP × MR.
  • Supply of labour: working conditions, qualifications, migration, geographic mobility.
  • Trade-union / professional-body bargaining power; monopsony (e.g. NHS for nurses).
  • Government intervention: National Living Wage, public-sector pay review bodies.
  • Application to chosen occupation (e.g. nurses, HGV drivers, software engineers): specific supply constraints (training time ~4 years for nurses; Tier 2 visa caps for migrant labour); MRPL often hard to measure in public services.
  • Diagram expected: labour demand and supply with equilibrium wage; monopsony variant showing MCL > wage.
Evaluation (out of 10)
  • MRPL model requires competitive market assumption — breaks down in monopsony.
  • Non-pecuniary factors (vocation, security) matter for nursing.
  • Short vs. long run: training lags mean shortages persist.
  • Public vs. private sector dynamics differ fundamentally.
  • Judgement: for occupations with long training pipelines and monopsony employers (nursing), supply-side and institutional factors dominate; for high-skilled private-sector roles (software engineers), MRPL and global labour mobility dominate.

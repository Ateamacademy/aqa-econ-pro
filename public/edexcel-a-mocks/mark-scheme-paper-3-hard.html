Question 1 — UK labour-market tightness case study

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
1(a)Tight labour market + Beveridge ratio:
  • Tight labour market: more vacancies than available unemployed workers; employers compete on wages.
  • In 2022: vacancy rate ≈ 4%; unemployment rate ≈ 3.8%.
  • V/U ratio = 4 / 3.8 ≈ 1.05 — vacancies exceed unemployment, historically exceptional.
  • Normal pre-pandemic V/U ≈ 0.4–0.6 → 2022 was approximately 2× normal level.
  • Implication: exceptional upward wage pressure likely; standard Phillips curve logic suggests wage growth.

1(b)Micro consequence of minimum-wage rise in tight market:
  • Already-high wage growth in low-paid sectors; minimum wage may be 'bite-reducing' (less binding than in slack markets).
  • Monopsony effects larger in concentrated local labour markets (care homes, logistics depots) — minimum wage can raise employment as well as wages.
  • Price pass-through: in services sector with low import competition, prices rise → contributes to inflation.
  • Substitution effects: capital investment accelerated (self-service kiosks, automation).
  • Productivity channel: wage compression forces firms to raise productivity or exit.
  • Supply: labour-supply participation rises (reservation wage channel).

1(d)Rising economic inactivity raises equilibrium NAIRU:
  • NAIRU = non-accelerating-inflation rate of unemployment; the rate of unemployment consistent with stable inflation.
  • Mechanism 1: reduced effective labour supply → tighter labour market at any given headline unemployment rate → more wage pressure at given u → higher NAIRU.
  • Mechanism 2: skills/health-related inactivity leaves remaining workforce mis-matched with vacancies → higher structural unemployment.
  • Mechanism 3: reduced labour-force participation narrows the pool of available substitutes, increasing bargaining power of employed 'insiders'.
  • Evidence: UK long-term inactivity rose by ~0.7m since 2019; widely cited contributor to sticky wage growth 2021–2024.
  • Macroeconomic consequence: MPC must set tighter policy to achieve the same inflation target.
  • Counter: if inactivity reverses (health recovery, better work-incentives), NAIRU can fall again.
  • Evaluation: mechanism is real but magnitude debated; estimates range 0.2–0.5 pp rise in NAIRU.

Question 2 — Skilled visa expansion + adult retraining — evaluate

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
2Indicative content:
  • Micro (labour market): expanded visa quotas increase labour supply in skilled sectors (health, engineering, ICT). Wage pressure eased; productivity may rise via skills.
  • Retraining raises domestic labour productivity and supply in mid-skilled sectors; reduces mismatch unemployment.
  • Macro (AD): immigrant workers raise population → higher C, I; AD shifts right.
  • Macro (AS): skilled workforce raises LRAS; trend growth rate rises.
  • Effect on inflation: expanded supply eases wage-push inflation pressure.
  • Fiscal: higher immigration raises tax receipts and spending; net typically positive for working-age immigration.
  • Productivity: evidence from OECD studies suggests skilled immigration raises TFP (spillover / knowledge transfer).
  • Political economy: public-services pressure (housing, NHS) a political constraint.
  • Retraining effectiveness: UK adult-skills spending has historically been low; implementation challenge.
  • Trade-offs: skilled visas may reduce incentive to train domestically; retraining programmes often have low completion rates.
  • Evaluation: combined approach (visas + retraining) plausibly net positive, but depends heavily on quality of retraining programme and integration support. Magnitude of effect is modest unless scale is large.

Question 3 — Critical minerals supercycle case study

QAnswer / Indicative contentMarksAO
3(a)Tripling of critical-mineral prices — cost structure of battery EV manufacturers:
  • Lithium, cobalt, nickel = key battery-cell inputs; battery pack ≈ 30–40% of EV BOM cost.
  • Tripling of mineral prices raises per-unit battery cost substantially (>50% increase in battery cost).
  • Shift of short-run AC curve upwards; MC rises → output falls at profit-max price, prices rise.
  • Cross-price elasticity: complements to EVs (chargers) demand falls; substitutes (ICE cars) demand rises.
  • Firms' responses: price pass-through, product mix changes (smaller batteries, LFP over NMC chemistry), vertical integration, hedging.
  • Diagrammatic treatment (cost/revenue) shows profit squeeze.
  • Innovation response: R&D in sodium-ion chemistries, recycling, alternative materials.

3(b)Commodity-exporting EM facing sharp price fall:
  • Micro: mining firms face revenue shock; some mines uneconomic and close.
  • Macro: export revenue falls → terms of trade deteriorate, currency depreciates.
  • Fiscal: mining royalties and profit taxes fall → widening fiscal deficit.
  • Balance of payments: widening CAD, foreign-exchange reserves drawn down.
  • Output: Dutch-disease reversal — non-mining tradables sector may recover as currency weakens.
  • Labour market: direct mining layoffs; knock-on effects in supporting sectors.
  • Inflation: imported inflation via currency depreciation; may offset deflationary demand effect.
  • Financial stability: dollar-denominated debt becomes more expensive in local-currency terms (if currency weakens).
  • Sovereign credit: rating downgrades; higher borrowing costs.
  • Policy response: counter-cyclical fiscal if buffers exist; IMF programme if not.
  • DRC / Chile specifics: DRC has limited fiscal buffers; Chile has sovereign-wealth buffers (copper stabilisation fund).

3(c)Evaluate strategic industrial policy (stockpiles, domestic-content rules, extraction subsidies):
  • Theoretical basis: market failure when private returns to extraction/processing diverge from social returns (strategic security, scale externalities).
  • Infant-industry argument: domestic processing capacity can become competitive over time if initially protected.
  • Strategic stockpiles internalise national-security externality; similar to petroleum reserve arguments.
  • Against: protectionism / industrial policy has poor historical track record (Japan & Korea selective success; India poor record).
  • Subsidy waste: deadweight losses if firms would have invested anyway; risk of 'picking losers'.
  • International-trade frictions: risk of WTO disputes; trade-diversion, retaliation.
  • Opportunity cost: subsidies mean less public spending on other priorities (health, education).
  • Friend-shoring & 'de-risking': reduced efficiency cost of over-diversification if genuine security threat exists.
  • Evidence on scale: EU Critical Raw Materials Act targets, IRA's $369bn green provisions — effectiveness still unknown.
  • Market-based alternatives: carbon pricing, targeted R&D tax credits, public-private research consortia.
  • Global coordination: strategic-reserves allocated efficiently through international cooperation rather than national duplication.
  • Distributional: costs fall on consumers (higher prices), benefits concentrated in supported sectors.
  • Judgement: case for some intervention in genuine strategic inputs exists, but blanket industrial policy is costly and risks trade wars; most efficient response is targeted measures with clear security rationale, combined with international cooperation.

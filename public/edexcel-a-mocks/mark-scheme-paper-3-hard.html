Question 1(b) – Using a cost and revenue diagram, discuss the likely microeconomic effects on a Big Four bank of continued market-share growth by digital challenger banks (12 marks) MICRO ONLY

Diagram required: Big Four bank cost/revenue with higher AC reflecting branch infrastructure and legacy IT (£150 vs. £30/customer/year); rising PED as customers gain alternatives; profit-maximising Q shifts; supernormal profit area shrinks.

KAA (out of 8)
  • Existing accounts (60% Big Four share) produce recurring fee and float income.
  • Flow competition (40% challenger share of new accounts) threatens long-run share.
  • Customer inertia key: only 3% of UK current-account holders switched in 2023.
  • Challengers' 80% cost advantage (£30 vs. £150) is structural, not copyable without branch closure.
  • Big Four compensate via lending, wealth, business banking — not pure current-account play.
Evaluation (out of 4)
  • Short-run: minimal profit impact due to stock inertia.
  • Medium-run: forced investment in app/service; some cost rises.
  • Long-run: if challengers sustain flow share, Big Four share shrinks, revenue pressured.
  • Defensive strategies: acquisitions (Virgin/Nationwide precedent); cross-subsidy; branch closures.
  • Judgement: Big Four profitability robust short-run due to stock inertia, but under structural threat if flow competition persists. Most likely outcome: margin compression and consolidation of weaker mid-tier players.

Question 1(c) – Examine two ways in which capital requirements for UK banks may act as a barrier to entry (8 marks)

  • Minimum-capital thresholds: Basel 3.1 CET1 minimum 4.5% of RWAs + buffers totalling 10%+ means entrants need substantial upfront equity. For a bank aiming at £1bn balance sheet, initial capital required runs to £100m+.
  • Compliance fixed costs: Monzo's £47m compliance spend on £880m revenue (5%) is a material overhead. Large banks spread this over much larger revenue bases — economies of scale in regulation.
  • Risk-weighting complexity: Basel 3.1 requires internal-ratings-based or standardised approaches that demand specialised risk-modelling expertise.
  • Supervisory relationships: PRA intensity and scrutiny create timing barrier (Revolut's three-year licence process).
  • Stress-testing: larger banks' ability to absorb stress-test losses is a barrier for smaller firms.
  • Application: 2024 consolidation (Virgin/Nationwide, Co-op/Coventry) reflects the fixed-cost hurdle of regulation.

Question 1(d) – Evaluate the case for tightening UK capital requirements beyond Basel 3.1 (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Externality argument: bank failure imposes social costs far exceeding private costs (2008 GFC ~20% of UK GDP; SVB/Credit Suisse 2023).
  • Capital requirements as Pigouvian instrument to internalise systemic externality.
  • Cost to banks: higher capital raises required return on equity; less lending at given cost of capital.
  • Market structure: capital rules favour scale; further consolidation.
  • Diagrams: externality diagram with MSC above MPC; or firm-level cost/revenue.
KAA — MACRO
  • Credit supply and AD: tighter requirements → less lending → lower AD short-run.
  • Financial stability: reduces probability/severity of future crises → fewer negative AD shocks.
  • Monetary-policy effectiveness: better-capitalised banks transmit policy more reliably.
  • Fiscal linkage: fewer bailouts → lower contingent fiscal liabilities.
  • Long-run productivity: stable banking underpins business investment.

Evaluation:

  • Short-run cost: tighter rules reduce credit growth; UK lending already slow.
  • Distributional: small business lending particularly sensitive — affects regional economies.
  • Diminishing returns: marginal social gains beyond Basel 3.1 may be small.
  • Alternative tools: macroprudential (sectoral risk weights, LTV limits) may be more targeted.
  • International coordination: UK unilateral tightening risks regulatory arbitrage.
  • Evidence: 2023 SVB/Credit Suisse failures suggest Basel 3.1 alone may not eliminate contagion.
  • Judgement: targeted further tightening in specific areas (systemic-importance surcharges, liquidity) justified given residual systemic risk; broad-based increases risk disproportionate macroeconomic costs with diminishing marginal social returns. Macroprudential tools and stronger resolution frameworks offer better risk-return trade-off than capital alone.

Question 1(e) – Evaluate whether Open Banking and similar policies represent an effective response to Big Four dominance (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Open Banking theory: reduces switching costs via portable data; increases PED for incumbents' services.
  • Competition framework targets specific market-failure channel (switching costs / status-quo bias).
  • Behavioural barriers: anchoring, loss aversion, status-quo bias limit even zero-cost switching.
  • Evidence of uptake: 12% of Big Four customers using Open Banking — modest.
  • Market power of data: who owns account data; competition in adjacent markets (credit scoring, PFM apps).
KAA — MACRO
  • Fintech investment: Open Banking creates ecosystem for start-ups; UK fintech sector ~250k jobs.
  • Credit allocation efficiency: better data → better lending decisions → higher productivity.
  • Consumer welfare: switching gains concentrated in better-informed minority.
  • Financial inclusion: Open Banking can help thin-file customers access credit.
  • Systemic risk: data-based lending models can produce correlated behaviour.

Evaluation:

  • Evidence on outcomes: actual switching rates remain very low (3% per year); behavioural barriers sticky.
  • Structural vs. behavioural constraints: Open Banking addresses information friction not deeper inertia.
  • International comparison: EU PSD2 similar outcomes — modest rather than transformative.
  • Alternative policies: default-switching, “active renewal” requirements (as in energy retail) may have larger effects.
  • Successful challengers (Monzo, Starling) rely less on Open Banking than on brand and product quality.
  • Judgement: Open Banking valuable enabling policy — removed a legal/technical barrier and fostered fintech ecosystem. But not the principal driver of increased competition: challenger emergence is more about app quality, brand, cost-to-serve than Open Banking per se. More effective response combines Open Banking with behavioural defaults, outcomes-based regulation, continued structural separation pressure. Open Banking is necessary but insufficient.

Question 2(b) – Examine two factors that have driven the rise in UK debt interest costs (8 marks)

  • Higher yields: 10-year gilt yield rose from 0.85% to 4.05% — a ~3.2pp increase. With debt of ~£2.6tn, a 3pp rise in effective interest rate adds ~£78bn/year once the stock is fully re-priced.
  • Higher debt stock: debt/GDP rose from 84.6% to 98.9% — more principal on which to pay interest.
  • Inflation-linked gilts: ~25% of UK gilts are index-linked; surging CPI (11.1% peak) triggered very high interest on this portion, spiking to £111bn in 2022–23.
  • Composition shift: BoE's QT has moved more gilt holdings to private investors demanding market yields.
  • End of QE cushion: during QE, BoE held ~£895bn of gilts effectively interest-free to Treasury. QT reverses this: Treasury now pays full market yield plus indemnifies BoE for QT losses (£19bn in 2024).
  • Maturity profile: UK has longer-dated debt than most peers — good for stability but means yield rises take years to feed through fully.

Question 2(c) – Discuss the likely microeconomic and macroeconomic effects on UK households, firms and the UK economy of a sudden 1pp rise in UK gilt yields when growth is below trend (12 marks) MICRO + MACRO

Microeconomic
  • Household cost of borrowing: re-fixing mortgages +£150–£300/month; affordability falls.
  • Firm cost of capital: corporate bond yields rise in parallel; marginal NPV projects fail.
  • Pension funds and insurers: gilt holdings suffer mark-to-market losses; solvency ratios affected.
  • Housing market at firm level: house-builders see slower sales, delayed site starts.
  • Bank balance-sheet: banks hold gilts for liquidity; paper losses affect capital ratios.
  • Sector heterogeneity: capital-intensive / leveraged firms hit hardest.
Macroeconomic (AD/AS)
  • AD shifts left (AD1 → AD2); starting below trend, output gap widens.
  • Transmission: higher yields → higher cost of credit → ↓C + ↓I.
  • Debt dynamics: UK debt ~£2.6tn; 1pp rise adds ~£26bn/year over 5–10 years.
  • Exchange rate: if credit-risk premium, sterling may depreciate; if rate-differential, sterling may appreciate.
  • Fiscal-monetary interaction: rising debt-interest squeezes discretionary spending → pro-cyclical tightening.
  • Potential hysteresis: lost investment and skills → LRAS damage.

Evaluation:

  • Starting position matters: below-trend means any AD fall deepens output gap.
  • Transmission lag: fixed-rate mortgages mean household impact takes 2–5 years.
  • Self-fulfilling dynamic: 2022 “mini-budget” episode illustrates risk.
  • Counter: reserve-currency status, LDI reforms, post-2022 institutional framework provide ballast.
  • Judgement: micro effects concentrated on mortgage holders, leveraged firms, pension schemes, gilt-holding banks — can be substantial; macro effects in below-trend starting position seriously damaging and risk hysteresis. Net impact could be 0.5–1pp reduction in real GDP growth over 1–2 years.

Candidates must cover both micro AND macro for L3.

Question 2(d) – Evaluate whether the Bank of England should suspend Quantitative Tightening to reduce the fiscal burden of debt-interest costs (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • QT sales: increases supply of gilts to private investors; pushes yields up.
  • Secondary markets: liquidity and market-making costs in gilt-repo affected.
  • Banks: larger gilt holdings if QT reduces central-bank reserves aggressively.
  • Competition in gilt auctions: changing structure of primary and secondary markets.
  • Diagrams: partial-equilibrium gilt market showing supply rise and price fall.
KAA — MACRO
  • Direct fiscal cost: BoE indemnity payments from Treasury = £19bn in 2024.
  • Indirect fiscal cost: QT raises gilt yields → higher debt-interest on future borrowing.
  • Monetary-policy space: QT restores central-bank balance-sheet headroom for future QE.
  • Financial stability: oversized BoE balance sheet creates distortions.
  • Inflation fighting: QT complements Bank Rate.

Evaluation:

  • Fiscal case: £19bn/year material given 99% debt-to-GDP.
  • Counter: suspension may raise inflation expectations — offsetting direct saving.
  • Passive vs. active QT: pausing active sales (BoE's 2026 plan) less disruptive than full suspension.
  • International precedent: Fed, ECB, BoJ all doing QT; unilateral UK suspension looks like fiscal dominance.
  • Central bank independence: suspension on fiscal grounds compromises operational autonomy.
  • Time-consistency: suspension now → investors expect future crisis-response suspensions → higher credit risk pricing.
  • Judgement: full suspension not justified — it would undermine monetary policy space, central bank credibility, and ultimately may not save fiscal cost if risk premia rise. Current pace of active sales open to legitimate debate; slower path (already announced for 2026) strikes better balance. Policy tension between monetary and fiscal objectives should be resolved through gradualism, not suspension.

Question 2(e) – Evaluate the view that “the UK's high debt-to-GDP ratio is sustainable provided that r does not persistently exceed g” (25 marks) MICRO + MACRO

KAA — MICRO
  • Debt dynamics: d(Debt/GDP) ≈ primary deficit + (r − g) × Debt/GDP.
  • When r = g, debt ratio stable if primary balance zero.
  • Cost of debt at margin: 10-year gilt yield reflects market's assessment of default risk, inflation risk, term premium.
  • Growth fundamentals: labour force, capital, productivity — UK trend growth 1.3–1.5% real, ~4% nominal.
  • Private-sector crowding-out via loanable funds market.
  • Diagrams: partial-equilibrium gilt market; debt-dynamics chart.
KAA — MACRO
  • When r < g, debt ratio tends to fall even with modest primary deficit (2000s UK).
  • When r > g, debt ratio rises even with primary balance (1980s, 2011–13 Eurozone crisis).
  • UK's current r ≈ g balance is delicate — small shocks can reverse it.
  • Financing risk: UK rolls over ~15% of debt each year; loss of confidence causes rapid stress.
  • Independent central bank prevents “inflating away” debt.
  • Currency regime: UK borrows in own currency, floating rate — cannot be forced to default like Eurozone periphery.

Evaluation:

  • Accounting identity correct: if r = g permanently and primary balance zero, debt ratio stable. But fragile equilibrium.
  • Historical: UK debt 250%+ in 1945 managed down by 1970s via g > r plus primary surplus.
  • 1970s stagflation when g fell below r — debt ratio exploded.
  • Modern context: aging population raises long-run G, lowers long-run g → r > g more likely.
  • Confidence effects: sustainability depends on market perception, not just arithmetic; 2022 “mini-budget” showed rapid re-pricing.
  • International comparison: Japan (250%+) has r < g and stable dynamics; Italy (140%) lives on knife-edge.
  • Primary balance matters: UK running 3–4% primary deficits adds to debt even if (r − g) is zero.
  • Judgement: view partially correct but dangerously incomplete. Debt ratio stability requires EITHER small primary balance AND r ≤ g, OR primary surplus large enough to offset (r − g). With UK currently running primary deficits and (r − g) near zero, trajectory is gradual debt-ratio rise. View ignores non-linearity: markets may tolerate 99% debt/GDP but demand much higher yields at 120%. Reasonable fiscal headroom requires primary balance to move toward zero or small surplus, not just (r − g) ≤ 0.

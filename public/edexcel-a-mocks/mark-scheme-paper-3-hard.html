General Marking Guidance

All candidates must receive the same treatment. Examiners must mark the first candidate in exactly the same way as they mark the last. Mark schemes should be applied positively. Candidates must be rewarded for what they have shown they can do rather than penalised for omissions.

Crossed-out work should be marked unless the candidate has replaced it with an alternative response. All the marks on the mark scheme are designed to be awarded. Examiners should always award full marks if deserved, i.e. if the answer matches the mark scheme.

Indicative content (candidates may include any of the following — this list is not exhaustive and other valid points should be credited):

  • Micro: subsidies distort market structure; picking winners risks lock-in; infant-industry argument may apply to specific sectors.
  • Firm location: marginal subsidies may tip decisions to UK, especially in supply-chain-intensive sectors.
  • Competition effects: domestic firms compete with subsidised foreign entrants.
  • Macro: fiscal cost (~£100bn over 10y to match scale); debt sustainability concern.
  • Trade: reduces import dependence but may trigger WTO disputes or retaliation.
  • Long-run growth: if effective, raises potential output via TFP in clean sectors.
  • Alternative: cost-effectiveness through carbon pricing + targeted R&D rather than broad subsidies.
  • Evaluation: depends on whether UK's comparative advantage lies in manufacturing (unlikely) or services / finance / R&D (more likely).

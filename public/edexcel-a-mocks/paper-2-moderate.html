Extract A
UK recovery and policy dilemmas

The UK economy has stabilised after the inflation shock of 2022–2023. By mid-2025, CPI inflation was close to the Bank of England's 2% target, but growth remained sluggish. Real GDP grew just 0.9% in 2024, and the Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) forecast 1.3% in 2025 – well below the pre-2008 trend.

The Bank of England cut the base rate from a peak of 5.25% in August 2024 to 4.50% by mid-2025, arguing that restrictive monetary policy could now be eased without reigniting inflation. However, services inflation remained sticky at around 4–5%, raising concerns that domestic wage pressures could prevent inflation from returning to target.

On the fiscal side, the government faces tight constraints. Public debt exceeds 99% of GDP, and debt interest payments absorbed £105 billion in 2024–25. The Chancellor announced a package of supply-side measures: extending the full-expensing investment allowance, reforming planning to accelerate housebuilding and energy infrastructure, and expanding the apprenticeship levy. Critics argue the package lacks demand-side support at a time when growth remains weak.

The UK's current account deficit widened to 3.5% of GDP in 2024 as the trade in goods deficit persisted. Services exports (financial services, education, digital) grew but did not offset weaker goods exports to the EU. Sterling's effective exchange rate has been broadly stable at levels about 15% below pre-2016 averages.

(Source: adapted from https://www.ft.com/content/uk-economy-policy-2025)

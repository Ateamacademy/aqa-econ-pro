Extract A
UK housing affordability crisis

The UK housing market has become one of the most strained in the developed world. The ratio of average house price to average earnings in England reached 8.6 in 2025, up from 4.6 in 2000. In London the ratio now exceeds 13.

High prices reflect persistently weak supply. Annual new-build completions have run well below the 300,000 target set in the 2019 Conservative manifesto, falling to an estimated 164,000 in 2025 as rising mortgage rates and construction costs squeezed developer economics. Private renters now devote around 34% of their post-tax income to rent, the highest share on record.

(Source: adapted from Resolution Foundation, 2025)

Extract B
Rate cuts and housing demand

Between August 2024 and the end of 2025 the Bank of England cut Bank Rate from 5.25% to 3.75% in five 0.25 percentage-point steps. The Monetary Policy Committee judged that services-sector inflation had returned to target and that the risks to output were tilted to the downside.

Mortgage applications rose by 22% year-on-year in Q4 2025 as lower rates repaired buyer confidence. Nationwide reported that UK house prices rose by 2.0% in 2025 after falling 2.6% in 2023, marking the gentlest correction-and-recovery cycle on record. Critics argue the rate cuts risk reigniting the housing market without addressing the underlying supply shortage.

(Source: adapted from Bank of England press briefing, 2025)

Extract C
A proposed planning reform

In 2025 the Labour government published a White Paper proposing the most comprehensive reform of the English planning system in a generation. Key measures include: mandatory housing targets for local authorities; a presumption in favour of development on poor-quality green-belt ("grey-belt") land near transport hubs; and streamlined consents for small brownfield sites.

Supporters including the Centre for Policy Studies argue the reforms could add 1.5 million homes over a decade and reduce real house prices by around 10%. Opponents including the Countryside Charity argue that weakening green-belt protection would damage biodiversity and that the Treasury projections overstate the elasticity of supply response to planning liberalisation.

(Source: adapted from The Guardian, 2025)

Extract A
The shape of the UK recovery

The UK economy suffered a sharp contraction of almost 10% in real output during 2020 as a result of COVID-19 lockdowns — the largest annual fall in recorded modern history. Rapid vaccine roll-out and large-scale fiscal support (including the furlough scheme) enabled a strong initial rebound in 2021, but growth slowed again as supply-chain disruption and the energy-price shock of 2022 fed through into sharply higher consumer prices.

By the end of 2025, UK real GDP remained only slightly above its pre-pandemic peak. Commentators have suggested that the UK recovery has been unusually weak compared with the United States and several major European economies. Low productivity growth, persistent labour-market disengagement and subdued business investment are commonly cited as constraints on the recovery.

(Source: adapted from Office for National Statistics and Institute for Fiscal Studies analysis, 2025)

Extract B
Monetary and fiscal policy since the pandemic

The Bank of England responded to the pandemic by cutting the Bank Rate to a record low of 0.1% and launching a major round of quantitative easing. When inflation surged in 2022, the Monetary Policy Committee reversed course, raising the Bank Rate sharply to 5.25% by mid-2023. As inflation fell in 2024 and 2025, the Bank began cautiously lowering the Bank Rate again.

Fiscal policy has been more constrained. High debt-to-GDP ratios, close to 100%, and elevated borrowing costs have limited the scope for tax cuts or major increases in public spending. The government has relied heavily on supply-side measures, including planning reforms, apprenticeship expansion and investment zones, to try to raise trend growth.

(Source: adapted from Bank of England Monetary Policy Report and HM Treasury budget documents, 2025)

Extract C
Labour market and productivity puzzles

Despite sluggish output growth, the UK unemployment rate has remained low by historical standards. Economists have focused on two features of the post-pandemic labour market: a significant rise in long-term sickness — an estimated 2.8 million working-age adults are inactive because of ill-health — and persistently weak productivity growth, with output per hour worked barely changing over the past decade.

Some analysts argue that these two trends are linked: inactive workers reduce effective labour supply, which in turn raises wage pressure, discourages investment in labour-saving technology and dampens long-run productivity growth. Others emphasise factors such as under-investment in public capital and skills, weak competition in services, and uncertainty arising from Brexit.

(Source: adapted from Bank of England and Resolution Foundation research, 2025)
